# Queue-Aware Graph Reinforcement Learning for UAV-ISAC-Assisted Maritime Data Collection 

Bohan Li, Member, IEEE , Min Ye, Haochen Liu, Yongkang Gong, Member, IEEE , Ning Gao, Member, IEEE , Jie Nie, Member, IEEE , Pei Xiao, Senior Member, IEEE , Xiuzhen Cheng, Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —This paper studies high-altitude platform (HAP)-assisted sparse cooperative integrated sensing and communication (ISAC) for UAV-enabled ocean monitoring. A fleet of rotary-wing UAVs senses drifting buoys, collects their monitoring data, and reports local posterior estimates to a HAP that performs fusion and sparse cooperation control. The model explicitly accounts for a spatially correlated sea-patch field, patch-aware buoy dynamics, RCS- and clutter-aware echo sensing, fused posterior Cramér-Rao bounds (PCRBs), and propulsion-energy-limited UAV mobility. The long-horizon objective is cast as a queue-weighted buffered-collection Markov decision process rather than instantaneous throughput, where each buoy maintains a backlog of buffered observations. The resulting long-horizon design is formulated as a mixed discrete-continuous problem with sensing, communication, mobility, safety, buffered-collection, and onboard-energy constraints. To address the combinatorial association component without replacing learning by a deterministic optimizer, we propose a structured feasible-association graph-MARL framework. A heterogeneous graph encoder produces candidate-edge logits, and a masked sequential b-matching policy samples legal UAV-buoy associations while exactly satisfying UAV-load and buoy-cluster constraints. A MAPPO-style training procedure, an independent queue-state value critic, and a consistency-verification protocol are then specified to support reproducible training. Simulation results on congested maritime scenarios show that the proposed policy improves the cumulative queue-weighted collection utility by about 106% over the rate-driven deterministic decoder, maintains a large margin across sea-state sweeps and medium-to-heavy traffic loads, and transfers to larger networks without fine-tuning. 

Index Terms —Integrated sensing and communication, maritime monitoring, UAV networks, graph neural networks, multi-agent reinforcement learning.

1 INTRODUCTION 

Ocean monitoring networks underpin environmental sensing, maritime safety, and the blue economy, relying on dense fleets of low-cost surface buoys and sensors to report oceanographic observations over wide, infrastructure-sparse sea areas [1]. The lack of reliable offshore backhaul and the low transmit power of such terminals make timely data delivery difficult, which motivates aerial relays that can approach the terminals on demand [2, 3]. Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are especially attractive here because their controllable mobility lets them establish strong line-of-sight uplinks to dispersed buoys and collect their buffered ob-servations efficiently. In parallel, integrated sensing and commu-nication (ISAC), which reuses a common waveform and aperture for both functions, has emerged as a key sixth-generation (6G) capability [4, 5]. Endowing maritime UAVs with ISAC lets a single platform localize drifting buoys and collect their data within one resource budget, a synergy well matched to the dual sensing-and-collection nature of ocean monitoring [6].

Realizing maritime UAV-ISAC, however, makes UAV tra-jectory optimization and resource allocation substantially harder than in terrestrial deployments. Sensing accuracy, uplink rate, propulsion energy, and the sparse cooperation pattern among UAVs and buoys are all tightly coupled through the UAV positions, so motion, sensing-power allocation, and UAV-buoy association cannot be designed independently [7, 8]. The open-sea setting compounds this difficulty in ways that terrestrial models do not capture. Buoys drift continuously under waves and currents, the sea surface returns strong sea-state-dependent clutter that degrades echo sensing, and beam alignment must rely on pre-dicted rather than measured geometry [9, 10]. The long mission duration and the limited onboard energy of rotary-wing UAVs further make the task a long-horizon one, so myopic per-slot maximization of throughput or sensing quality can leave urgent buoy data uncollected [11]. These maritime-specific couplings call for a unified long-horizon ISAC-and-control design rather than a communication-only scheduling formulation, and they form the central challenge that this paper targets. 

1.1 Related Works 

1.1.1 UAV-Enabled ISAC 

UAV-enabled ISAC has been widely investigated as a way to exploit the controllable mobility and line-of-sight channels of aerial platforms for joint sensing and communication [12]. Representative designs jointly optimize the UAV maneuver and the transmit beamforming [7], maximize communication throughput under periodic sensing constraints [10], and plan cooperative multi-UAV trajectories with resource allocation to bound the 

> arXiv:2607.00324v1 [eess.SY] 1 Jul 2026 2

target-localization error [8]. Sensing has also been exploited to assist communication through predictive beam tracking of moving targets [13]. These works establish the value of jointly controlling motion and physical-layer resources. Other studies jointly design the UAV maneuver and the hybrid or digital beamforming to balance the sensing beampattern against the achievable rate, and cooperative multi-UAV designs further schedule trajectories and resources across platforms to enlarge sensing coverage and uplink capacity [14]. Across these efforts, aerial platforms are usually treated as energy-unconstrained over the planning window, and the data actually buffered at the served terminals is abstracted away, so the onboard-energy budget and the timeliness of long-horizon data clearance are not modeled. These works nonetheless consider quasi-static ground users or targets over land and optimize instan-taneous sensing or rate objectives, which leaves open the maritime regime and its long-horizon data-clearance demands. 

1.1.2 Maritime UAV-ISAC and Data Collection 

Motivated by this gap, a growing number of works bring UAVs into the maritime domain [2]. Energy-efficient trajectory and resource designs have been proposed for UAV-aided ocean monitoring and maritime data collection [3, 9, 15], and ISAC has recently been introduced into maritime emergency and monitoring networks [6, 16]. Related studies optimize shipboard maritime ISAC deployment [17], enhance maritime joint sensing and com-munication with reconfigurable surfaces [18, 19], and schedule cooperative UAV data collection to reduce the age of informa-tion [20]. These designs confirm the value of UAV-ISAC at sea. Other works extend maritime connectivity through high-altitude-platform and integrated satellite-air-ground architectures over the open sea [5], which motivates using a HAP as a coordinating fusion center rather than a mere relay. Most maritime studies, however, still decouple sensing from data collection and track a per-link rate or an aggregate volume rather than the per-buoy backlog. Most of them nevertheless retain instantaneous rate or sensing objectives and do not model the buffered, urgency-weighted clearance of buoy data under sparse multi-UAV coop-eration, which is exactly the regime an ocean-monitoring mission must operate in. 

1.1.3 Learning-Based Trajectory and Resource Optimiza-tion 

The non-convex and only partially observable nature of trajec-tory and resource optimization in these dynamic maritime scenar-ios limits conventional convex and alternating-optimization meth-ods, so machine-learning approaches have become an attractive alternative. Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has been applied to UAV-ISAC trajectory and resource control [14, 21, 22] and to multi-domain resource management across space-air-ground networks [23, 24]. Graph neural networks (GNNs) effectively exploit the relational structure of wireless networks [25], and their combination with multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) yields scalable permutation-aware policies for distributed wireless control [26, 27]. Graph-based learning has also been applied to multi-UAV cooperation, for instance cooperative multi-UAV trajectory design over a heterogeneous UAV-terminal graph [28] and heterogeneous-graph beamforming for cell-free maritime sys-tems [29]. Closest to our setting, a hierarchical graph-MARL framework has recently been proposed for multi-UAV ISAC, where a coordinating agent allocates bandwidth and user asso-ciation over a heterogeneous graph while distributed UAV agents optimize subcarriers, power, and trajectories [30]. Such learning-based ISAC designs typically target an instantaneous weighted sensing-rate objective and form the discrete association either by adding a soft penalty for infeasible choices or by repairing an infeasible association after it is sampled, which leaves room for a long-horizon formulation in which the sampled association is fea-sible by construction. More broadly, structured and permutation-equivariant graph policies have been shown to transfer across network sizes in wireless resource allocation. Our design inherits this graph-based scalability but departs from prior learning-based ISAC in two respects, namely, the objective is a long-horizon queue-weighted collection utility rather than an instantaneous sensing-rate sum, and the discrete association is sampled by a feasibility-preserving decoder so that the optimized and executed actions coincide. 

1.2 Contributions 

Motivated by these gaps, this paper develops a HAP-assisted maritime sparse cooperative ISAC framework whose long-horizon objective is the timely clearance of buffered buoy data and whose sparse UAV-buoy association is optimized by a feasibility-preserving graph policy. The main contributions are summarized as follows.  

> •

We design a HAP-coordinated UAV-ISAC model specifically for the maritime UAV data collection scenario, integrating a sea-patch environmental field, patch-aware Singer buoy dynamics, RCS- and clutter-aware echo sensing with fused PCRB and rotary-wing propulsion energy within a three-phase superframe. Unlike existing UAV-ISAC designs that maximize instantaneous throughput or sensing quality, we cast the long-horizon task as a queue-weighted buffered-collection MDP in which the uplink rate matters only through a rate-coupled service that clears urgency-weighted buoy backlogs. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to target the timely clearance of buffered buoy data with the aid of ISAC, and the resulting mixed discrete-continuous problem is recast as a coordinator-assisted decentralized partially observable Markov decision process (Dec-POMDP) over a physics-grounded het-erogeneous graph built at the HAP from only causal observable quantities.  

> •

We design a structured graph reinforcement learning policy for this sparse HAP-coordinated cooperation, in which a hetero-geneous graph encoder produces candidate-edge logits and a masked sequential b-matching decoder samples a legal UAV-buoy association that satisfies the UAV-load and buoy-cluster limits by construction. Its log-probability therefore equals that of the executed action, in contrast to prior graph reinforcement learning and MARL resource-allocation designs that form the association through soft penalties or after-the-fact repair and thus optimize an action that may differ from the one executed. The whole hierarchy is trained on-policy under a MAPPO-style CTDE procedure without imitation labels.  

> •

We conduct extensive simulations against deterministic and learning baselines, including tests on unseen scenario instances and on a larger network without any retraining, to validate the effectiveness and performance of the design. On the congested maritime scenario the learned policy raises the cumulative queue-weighted utility by 106 .2% over the rate-optimal deterministic decoder and surpasses both the one-step queue optimizer and the backlog-urgency greedy heuristic, 3HAP

The overall architecture and the centralized-training, decentralized-execution (CTDE) information flow of the proposed framework are summarized in Fig. 2. The environment then returns the realized reward r[t], and an independent queue-state critic Vϕ provides the value baseline to the MAPPO trainer, which updates the policy parameters Λ = {ψ, φ ℓ, θ loc }. The encoder, edge-logit head, and decoder reside at the HAP and run at both training and execution, since the HAP must produce the sparse association γ[t] every superframe, whereas the centralized queue-state critic Vϕ is used during training only and is discarded at execution. Under the CTDE paradigm, execution therefore needs only the HAP-side association modules and the shared local actor πθloc replicated across the UAVs, with no centralized critic in the loop. 5 SIMULATIONS AND RESULTS 

We evaluate the proposed framework on the HAP-assisted maritime monitoring scenario of Section 2. In the default con-figuration, a 2.5 km × 2.5 km sea region is partitioned into 

S = 24 sea patches and monitored by M = 6 rotary-wing UAVs at a fixed altitude of 50 m , which sense and collect buffered observations from K = 24 drifting buoys (one per patch) over 

T = 40 superframes of duration ∆T = 1 s . The sea-patch-correlated field drives the buoy drift, the RCS fluctuation, and the residual sea clutter, so the sensing geometry and the link quality evolve throughout the mission. The UAVs start at evenly spaced launch points covering the area and each buoy is initialized in its patch with a small random perturbation. The full physical and algorithmic parameters are listed in Table 1, while the panels that probe traffic load, sensing stringency, or network size vary these settings as stated in their subsections. Regarding the learning network structures, the heterogeneous UAV-buoy candidate graph is processed by a physics- and queue-grounded edge-conditioned message-passing encoder with a hid-den width of 64 and two message-passing rounds. The resulting candidate-edge embeddings are mapped to logits and decoded into a sparse association by the masked sequential b-matching sampler, which keeps every sampled association feasible by construction and exposes the exact log-probability of the executed action. Conditioned on the received directives, a parameter-shared local UAV actor outputs the continuous motion and sensing-power actions, and an independent queue-state critic provides the value baseline. The association and the local actor are trained jointly on-policy under the MAPPO paradigm (clipped PPO with generalized advantage estimation). During implementation, training runs for 

Nit = 120 iterations, each collecting Nep = 3 on-policy episodes and then reusing this batch for Nppo = 4 PPO epochs. 5.2.5 Generalization and Size Transfer 

To test whether the learned policy is tied to its training instances, Table 5 applies two transfer scenarios. In Scenario I, the policy is trained on four scenario instances and evaluated on three unseen instances of the same size ( M =6 , K=24 ). In Scenario II, the policy trained at M =6 , K=24 is evaluated, without any fine-tuning, on a larger network with M =8 and K=32 . As the results show, the proposed policy passes both tests, since averaged over three independent training repetitions it retains a +85 .6% margin over EP2 in Scenario I and a +132 .1% margin in Scenario II, where it even exceeds BU-Greedy on that network. Moreover, F-MLP stays below EP2 on the unseen same-size instances, dropping to a −30 .6% margin, and it cannot be evaluated at a larger size since its dimensions are hard-wired to 

M K . E-MLP transfers only marginally, ending barely above the deterministic decoder in Scenario II ( +5 .2% ). GNN-BR reaches a high mean utility but is unreliable, with a ±3.67 spread across runs (nearly ten times the proposed policy’s ±0.37 ) because one run collapses, which is the transfer-time symptom of the action-credit mismatch, as about 49% of its optimized actions are discarded by repair in Scenario II. By contrast, the proposed policy transfers while preserving exact feasibility, the lowest variance, and the highest mean margin ( 17 .52 versus GNN-BR’s 14 .63 ). This transferability is architectural rather than accidental, because all trainable parameters are shared per node type or per edge, the feasibility decoder is parameter-free and re-derives its masks from the new (M, K, D max , L max ), the features are dimensionless, and the only size-bound component, the centralized queue-state critic, is used during training only and discarded at execution under the CTDE paradigm. The bottom row of Table 5 shows that this scalability is computational as well, since the average per-decision inference latency grows only from 7.8 to 14 .9 ms as the network grows from Scenario I to Scenario II, well within the one-second superframe. 

6 CONCLUSION 

This paper studied HAP-assisted maritime sparse coopera-tive ISAC under a queue-weighted buffered-collection objective, where rotary-wing UAVs sense drifting buoys and collect buffered observations through sparse cooperative uplinks. We cast the per-superframe sparse association as a feasibility-preserving stochastic 

b-matching over a heterogeneous coordination graph, and trained it jointly with the local motion and sensing-power actors by on-policy MAPPO without any imitation labels. On the congested maritime scenario the learned policy raised the cumulative queue-weighted utility by 106 .2% over the exact rate-driven decoder, surpassed both the one-step queue optimizer and the backlog-urgency greedy heuristic, and kept a large margin across the sea-state sweep and under medium-to-heavy traffic where queue coupling is binding, while in light traffic the deterministic decoder remains competitive. Without any fine-tuning, the policy also transferred to a larger network, retaining the highest mean margin and the lowest variance among all learning designs. Finally, while this work assumes an ideal, rate-unconstrained HAP-UAV control link to focus on the queue-aware ISAC data col-lection, real-world maritime deployments may experience control-link fading, latency, or packet loss under extreme weather con-ditions. Future extensions will investigate the joint design of the sparse cooperation policy and the reliable delivery of HAP direc-tives under constrained, fading, and rate-limited control channels to further enhance the robustness of the framework.
