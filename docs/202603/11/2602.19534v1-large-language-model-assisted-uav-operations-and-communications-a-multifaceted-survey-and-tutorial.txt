# Large Language Model-Assisted UAV Operations and Communications: A Multifaceted Survey and Tutorial 

Yousef Emami, Senior Member, IEEE, Hao Zhou, Senior Member, IEEE, Radha Reddy, Atefeh Hajijamali Arani, Biliang Wang, Kai Li, Senior Member, IEEE, Luis Almeida, Senior Member, IEEE, and Zhu Han, Fellow Member, IEEE 

Abstract —Uncrewed Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) are widely deployed across diverse applications due to their mobility and agility. Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) offer atransformative opportunity to enhance UAV intelligence beyond conventional optimization-based and learning-based approaches. By integrating LLMs into UAV systems, advanced environmental understanding, swarm coordination, mobility optimization, and high-level task reasoning can be achieved, thereby allowing more adaptive and context-aware aerial operations. This sur-vey systematically explores the intersection of LLMs and UAV technologies and proposes a unified framework that consolidates existing architectures, methodologies, and applications for UAVs. We first present a structured taxonomy of LLM adaptation techniques for UAVs, including pretraining, fine-tuning, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), and prompt engineering, along with key reasoning capabilities such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) and In-Context Learning (ICL). We then examine LLM-assisted UAV communications and operations, covering navigation, mis-sion planning, swarm control, safety, autonomy, and network management. After that, the survey further discusses Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) for human–swarm interaction, perception-driven navigation, and collaborative control. Finally, we address ethical considerations, including bias, transparency, accountability, and Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) strategies, and outline future research directions. Overall, this work positions LLM-assisted UAVs as a foundation for intelligent and adaptive aerial systems. 

Index Terms —Uncrewed Aerial Vehicles, Large Language Mod-els, 6G Networks, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Prompt En-gineering, In-Context Learning, Chain-of-Thought. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Uncrewed Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have become a salient en-abling technology across a wide range of applications, including environmental monitoring [1], public safety [2], transportation [3], agriculture [4], and infrastructure inspection [5]. For exam-ple, UAVs can track environmental changes, support emergency response during disasters, assess crop health, deliver parcels, and assist with traffic management. Due to their flexibility and mobility, UAVs provide efficient and cost-effective solutions in situations where conventional ground-based systems are limited or infeasible [6]. From a communication perspective, UAVs are increasingly important for enhancing wireless networks. They can serve as flying base stations to expand network coverage, especially in areas with sparse or damaged infrastructure [7], [8]. UAVs can also operate as relays [9], [10], collect data from ground devices [11], and provide edge computing capabilities in the

When multiple UAVs cooperate, they can form Flying Ad hoc Networks (FANETs) that self-organize and provide either extended range [14] or communication services over large geographical areas or in environments without pre-existing infrastructure. These capabilities make UAVs a critical com-ponent of wireless backhauling, smart city applications, and future mobile communication systems [15]. Advancements in cost efficiency, operational flexibility, and dynamic deployment capabilities are driving the rapid expan-sion of the UAV ecosystem. Modern UAV platforms are in-creasingly equipped with high-resolution cameras and precision sensors, enabling large-scale and cost-effective data acquisition. As a result, UAVs have become highly valuable for numerous civilian applications across diverse industries [16]. The global UAV market was valued at USD 36.41 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach USD 125.91 billion by 2032, with a Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR) of 17.3% [17]. This rapid market growth underscores the growing demand for intelligent UAV-assisted solutions. In parallel with these developments, Artificial Intelligence (AI) has become a salient enabler for enhancing the autonomy and efficiency of UAV-assisted communication systems. AI techniques are widely used to optimize UAV mobility, resource allocation, and scheduling, allowing UAVs to adapt dynamically to both aerial and ground operations. These approaches support large-scale network management by enabling efficient and au-tomated data collection. The adoption of AI is primarily driven by the inherent complexity and highly dynamic nature of UAV operating environments, where accurate analytical modeling is often infeasible. Additionally, UAV mobility creates high-dimensional state and action spaces, whereas limited sensing capabilities and intermittent connectivity result in incomplete or outdated information about ground devices, including bat-tery levels, energy availability, and channel conditions. These challenges make data-driven and learning-based solutions par-ticularly well-suited to UAV network optimization [17]. Recently, AI has advanced significantly with the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs), which provide enhanced capabilities in language understanding, generation, and reason-ing. Building on decades of progress in natural language pro-cessing and Machine Learning (ML), LLMs have become a ma-jor driver of recent developments in AI-assisted systems. A. Why Combining UAVs and LLMs Matters 

The integration of UAVs with LLMs introduces a new level of intelligence that conventional control and optimization methods struggle to achieve. Optimization has long been cen-tral to UAV communications, such as resource allocation and trajectory design. These problems typically require selecting optimal solutions from discrete decision spaces to maximize objectives, such as throughput, under strict operational con-straints. Conventional optimization techniques, such as con-vex optimization and metaheuristic algorithms, lack sufficient adaptability in highly dynamic UAV environments. Although ML and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) techniques offer greater flexibility, they often exhibit limited generalization and a persistent simulation-to-reality gap, hindering their reliable deployment in real-world UAV scenarios. Compared with conventional ML-based optimization meth-ods, LLMs introduce a fundamentally different paradigm cen-tered on semantic reasoning rather than parameter tuning alone. While traditional ML and DRL approaches optimize numerical objectives through repeated interaction with simulated environ-ments, LLMs operate at a higher abstraction level, enabling structured reasoning over heterogeneous inputs and mission-level objectives. This capability allows LLMs to function not merely as optimizers, but as cognitive coordinators within UAV systems. Specifically, in UAV communications and networking scenarios, LLMs can interpret network logs, topology infor-mation, channel conditions, and mission constraints in natural language form, transforming raw telemetry into structured situational awareness. Instead of solving isolated optimization subproblems (e.g., power allocation or trajectory refinement), LLMs can synthesize cross-layer information to guide adaptive spectrum management, cooperative relay strategies, and swarm-level resource coordination. By reasoning over operational rules, safety constraints, and mission priorities, LLMs can generate context-aware decision strategies that complement lower-level control modules. Beyond communication optimization, LLMs enhance UAV autonomy by bridging perception, planning, and execution. Through multimodal integration, LLMs can align sensor data, map representations, and textual mission descriptions into a unified reasoning framework. This enables dynamic task de-composition, contingency planning, and collaborative swarm coordination under uncertain or partially observable conditions. Rather than relying solely on fixed reward functions, LLM-driven systems can adjust strategies based on evolving mission semantics and operator intent. In addition, this reduces the dependence on handcrafted rule sets and specialized pipelines and mitigates the need for multiple task-specific models. By unifying diverse data modalities—natural-language commands, telemetry streams, network metrics, and environmental seman-tics—LLMs provide a coherent operational layer that supports complex, multi-objective UAV missions. A defining feature of LLMs is In-Context Learning (ICL), which enables real-time adaptation to user requirements through feedback alone, without the computational overhead of retraining. This adaptability, combined with inherent scalability, allows LLMs to handle the combinatorial complexity of multi-UAV optimization by decomposing high-level, multi-objective missions into coherent sub-tasks and executable strategies. Furthermore, the rich semantic knowledge embedded in LLMs supports decision-making that closely aligns with human in-tuition and established operational doctrines. These reasoning capabilities also improve transparency and trust by enabling UAV systems to generate natural-language explanations of their actions. This explainability enables more intuitive communi-cation with human operators, reduces cognitive and opera-tional burdens, and allows non-expert users to control UAVs effectively. Ongoing advances in communication technologies, such as 6G and satellite internet, provide low-latency, high-bandwidth connectivity required to support real-time LLM-assisted UAV operations, thereby accelerating the transition toward intelligent, autonomous aerial systems. In the following, we present a unified abstraction at the system-level to explain how LLMs interact holistically with UAV communication, control, and decision-making. This is 

a macro-level methodological framework for understanding LLM–UAV integration that is organized into three layers : 

> •

At the highest layer, the cognitive and decision-making layer assigns LLMs the role of meta-controller, responsible for high-level reasoning and mission orchestration. Rather than operating on raw sensor data, the LLM processes symbolic and semantic representations of mission goals, environmental context, and system constraints. This layer translates UAV intent and abstract objectives into struc-tured task descriptions, enabling mission planning, task decomposition, constraint reasoning, and adaptive replan-ning in dynamic environments.  

> •

The perception layer mediates between cognition and execution by converting multimodal sensor inputs into semantically meaningful descriptions. Data from vision, LiDAR, radar, and spectrum sensors are fused into higher-level concepts such as objects, activities, behaviors, and inferred intent. LLMs, often combined with MLLMs, provide contextual interpretation, allowing the UAV to understand not only what is observed but also how it relates to mission objectives.  

> •

The control and communication layer, at the bottom, translate high-level plans and semantic decisions into exe-cutable actions, such as control commands, flight trajecto-ries, and communication policies. Execution outcomes and system states are abstracted and relayed to higher layers, forming a closed-loop learning and adaptation process. This layered abstraction unifies the diverse LLM-assisted meth-ods surveyed in this paper by framing LLMs as cross-layer cognitive integrators rather than isolated components. It clarifies how LLMs complement traditional UAV subsystems, enabling scalable autonomy, semantic reasoning, and adaptive decision-making in communication, control, and mission planning. 

B. Main Contributions 

This paper presents a comprehensive investigation of LLM-UAV interactions. It systematically examines how LLM-assisted techniques, such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and prompt engineering, are fundamentally transforming UAV operations by enhancing decision-making, enabling adap-tive path planning, and supporting real-time human–UAV in-teraction. In addition, we explore the paradigm shift from text-only LLMs to MLLMs, analyzing their architectures, training methodologies, and emerging applications in vision–language navigation, swarm coordination, and high-level mission plan-ning. After that, beyond technical advancements, we critically assess the ethical implications of integrating LLMs into UAV operation and communications, including privacy and data security, bias and fairness, transparency and accountability, and environmental sustainability. Finally, we present an LLM-aided UAV case study and identify the future research directions. Overall, this work serves as a comprehensive technical survey and tutorial, as well as an essential ethical guide for the evolving landscape of LLM-assisted autonomous UAV systems. The main contributions of this survey are the following:  

> •

Comprehensive synthesis and unified framework that systematically maps the convergence of LLMs and UAV systems across operational, communication, and ethical di-mensions. By consolidating existing fragmented research, it provides a unified framework and a clear overview of existing architectures, methodologies, and real-world ap-plications. In addition, we introduce a structured taxonomy of LLM approaches explicitly tailored to UAV challenges, encompassing pretraining, fine-tuning, RAG, and prompt engineering. This taxonomy clarifies the current research landscape and highlights promising directions for future development at the intersection of LLMs and UAVs.  

> •

In-depth discussion of MLLMs as a transformative enabler for next-generation UAV systems, detailing their core architectures, training paradigms, and emerging capa-bilities such as Multimodal Chain of Thought (MCoT) rea-soning and Multimodal ICL (M-ICL). We further demon-strate how MLLMs enable vision–language navigation and facilitate coordinated swarm control, underscoring their growing importance in complex perception, reasoning, and decision-making tasks for autonomous UAVs.  

> •

Rigorous analysis of the ethical challenges associated with deploying LLM-assisted UAV systems (LAUS), in-cluding risks related to bias and fairness, transparency and accountability in autonomous decision-making, and the environmental impact of LLMs. Beyond identifying these issues, the survey overviews related mitigation strategies, emphasizing LLM observability for systematic monitoring and debugging. Together, these recommendations establish a practical foundation for building trustworthiness, respon-sibility, and resilience in LLM-assisted UAV communica-tions.

This section presents LLM model architectures and investi-gates their training, adaptation, and deployment for UAV oper-ations. Training LLMs from scratch enables strong general ca-pabilities, but is elusive for UAV applications due to prohibitive computational, energy, and time costs. Fine-tuning pretrained LLMs is an efficient approach to adapt model architectures to UAV-specific tasks, such as mobility and swarm optimization. However, it requires careful control over overfitting and the use of labeled data. RAG further improves LLM deployment by integrating external knowledge sources with LLM architectures, enabling access to up-to-date operational data and airspace regulations without retraining. Prompt engineering provides a lightweight approach to leveraging existing LLM architectures for rapid deployment and human–UAV interaction, enabling flexible and resource-efficient task optimization. 

A. LLM Model Architectures 1) Encoder-Only Architectures: These are LLM architec-tures designed to generate rich contextual representations of input data. Their core mechanism is bidirectional self-attention, which enables them to capture a word’s context by considering all surrounding words. This capability makes them particularly effective for tasks that require deep language comprehension, such as understanding objects and scenes. The development of this architecture was pioneered by BERT, which introduced the masked language modeling objective. In UAV-related applications, encoder-only models have demonstrated strong performance in text understanding tasks. In this line, Silalahi et al. [28] propose a Transformer-based Named Entity Recognition (NER) framework for extracting forensic information from UAV flight logs. Using a manually annotated DJI dataset with six entity types, their BERT-based architecture achieved an F1 score of 91.348%, enabling efficient identification of forensically relevant entities. 

2) Encoder-decoder Architectures: These employ a three-module design to handle complex tasks: an encoder that pro-cesses the input, a decoder that generates the output, and a cross-attention mechanism that enables the decoder to focus on relevant parts of the input. This structured division of respon-sibilities often improves the sample and inference efficiency, making it well-suited for models such as T5, BART [29], and OpenBA [30]. The advanced text understanding and generation capabilities of the T5 language model provide a powerful means to enhance the autonomy and utility of UAVs significantly. By enabling natural language command interpretation and automated data summarization, T5 can streamline human-UAV interaction and convert raw sensor data into actionable intelligence, increasing efficiency in critical missions such as disaster response [20]. Encoder-decoder LLMs offer advantages in data efficiency, es-pecially when tasks require both understanding and generative capabilities [31]. 3) Decoder-Only Architectures: These are a foundational class of LLM architectures designed for generative tasks, producing human-like text that is coherent and contextually relevant [32]. Their core mechanism is causal (autoregressive) self-attention, which restricts the model to attending only to previous tokens in a sequence when generating the next word. This enables step-by-step text generation while main-taining contextual consistency. The modern era of decoder-only models began with the GPT series, which established the paradigm of pretraining on large-scale text corpora to achieve advanced language-generation capabilities. Today, decoder-only architectures underpin many influential Generative AI (GAI) systems, from research-focused models to leading commercial applications. In UAV-related applications, decoder-only LLMs have shown substantial potential for natural language interaction and con-trol. For example, Tazir et al. [33] integrated OpenAI’s Chat-GPT with the PX4/Gazebo UAV simulation platform, enabling intuitive UAV control through spoken or written commands. Their framework relies on prompt-engineered command gen-eration with a verification mechanism to ensure safety and reliability, achieving high command-generation accuracy in zero-shot settings and demonstrating the feasibility of user-friendly human–UAV interfaces. Additionally, Zhang et al. 

[34] introduce Open Pretrained Transformers (OPT), a suite of decoder-only models with up to 175 billion parameters. OPT-175B achieves performance comparable to GPT-3 while training with a significantly lower carbon footprint, thereby supporting reproducible research. In general, these studies high-light the versatility of decoder-only LLMs for natural-language-driven UAV control and automation, while also underscoring their reliance on large-scale training data. Overall, the architecture of an LLM, whether encoder-only, encoder-decoder, or decoder-only, directly affects how effec-tively it can leverage pre-training to acquire language un-derstanding, reasoning, and generative capabilities. Pretraining converts these architectural potentials into practical intelligence by exposing the model to large-scale general and domain-specific datasets, enabling it to develop emergent abilities essen-tial for UAV applications. The following subsection describes the pretraining process, data preparation strategies, and the challenges of adapting LLMs for UAV-specific contexts. 

B. LLM Pre-training 

The pretraining of LLMs primarily relies on next-token prediction, which gives models strong language comprehension, reasoning, and long-range dependency modeling capabilities. In UAV communications, pretraining typically uses two types of data, namely general corpora and UAV-specific datasets. General corpora (e.g., Wikipedia, technical blogs, and web content) provide linguistic diversity and support understand-ing of technical documentation, whereas specialized datasets focus on UAV- and aerial-specific knowledge. These include 

3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) standards, Request For Comments (RFC) documents, academic literature, network configuration logs, protocol specifications, simulation reports, and operational UAV repositories. Incorporating such domain-specific sources enhances the model’s understanding of UAV systems, communication architectures, and optimization tasks, making it better suited to UAV-oriented reasoning. Pretraining LLMs from scratch for UAV applications can be highly resource-intensive. UAV-relevant data, such as flight logs, sensor readings, communication traces, and environmen-tal information, require careful handling in domain-specific formats, including navigation equations, flight dynamics, and swarm coordination rules. Moreover, the computational cost of large-scale training poses significant challenges for real-time UAV systems and edge deployments. For instance, training a frontier-scale model such as LLaMA 3.1 (405B) requires tens of millions of GPU hours and incurs costs on the order of tens of millions of dollars [35]. Consequently, complete pretraining is often infeasible in UAV networks, underscoring the importance of fine-tuning pretrained models to achieve cost-effective and practical domain adaptation. 

C. LLM Fine-tuning 

Fine-tuning adapts a pretrained LLM to domain-specific tasks using relatively small datasets, significantly reducing data and computational requirements compared to training from scratch. Using representations learned during large-scale pretraining, fine-tuning enables efficient specialization for UAV networks, improving task performance while maintaining gen-eralization. This makes it a practical approach for deploying LLMs under UAV-specific constraints, such as limited compu-tation and dynamic operating environments. There are three main fine-tuning paradigms. Unsupervised fine-tuning adapts models using unlabeled domain text, im-proving domain familiarity but offering limited task precision. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) relies on labeled datasets and often yields strong performance, though annotation costs can be prohibitive in UAV communications. Instruction fine-tuning, commonly implemented through prompt engineering, guides models using natural-language instructions and reduces labeling requirements, although performance is highly dependent on prompt design. Broadly, the fine-tuning plays a critical role in adapting pretrained LLMs to UAV tasks by balancing per-formance, efficiency, and deployment feasibility [36]. As shown in Fig. 2, fine-tuning for UAV applications typically follows a structured pipeline encompassing UAV-specific data preparation, model initialization, training environ-ment configuration, parameter updates, evaluation, deployment, and long-term monitoring. Model parameters may be partially or fully updated, depending on resource constraints, using Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT) [37] to reduce over-head. Here, the PEFT adapts large pretrained models by freez-ing most parameters and updating only a small subset, such as adapters, low-rank matrices, or prompt embeddings. This strat-egy substantially reduces training cost, memory footprint, and convergence time while preserving pretrained knowledge and mitigating catastrophic forgetting. In UAV networks, fine-tuning supports task-specific adaptation for applications such as troubleshooting and configuration; however, rapidly evolving domain knowledge often necessitates combining fine-tuning with RAG to maintain up-to-date and context-aware performance.

RAG retrieves relevant information at inference time, allowing models to incorporate evolving standards, architectures, and transmission technologies critical to UAV networking. Access to authoritative sources such as 3GPP and IEEE standards, emerging network paradigms, and recent advances in signal processing significantly improve the factual accuracy and con-textual relevance of the generated outputs. By grounding responses in curated domain-specific corpora, RAG reduces hallucinations and outdated information without the prohibitive computational cost of training models from scratch or performing extensive fine-tuning. This makes RAG a practical and scalable approach for leveraging general-purpose LLMs in specialized UAV networking tasks [50]. More impor-tantly, evidence-grounded generation enhances trustworthiness by enabling traceable, policy-aligned, and verifiable decision-making. In safety-critical UAV networks, where accuracy, ac-countability, and compliance are essential, retrieval-assisted grounding underpins the responsible deployment of AI. E. Prompt Engineering: Strategies for UAV Intelligence 

Prompt engineering is the process of designing prompts to guide LLMs in generating accurate and useful outputs. As a reusable and adaptable methodology, prompt engineering supports structured problem-solving across diverse domains, improves understanding of LLM behavior, and enables few-shot learning by optimizing in-context prompts. It also plays an important role in the development of advanced UAV systems. As LLM technologies evolve, prompt engineering is expected to become increasingly important, offering finer control over model outputs and enabling new classes of applications. Creating effective prompts typically involves several key steps. First, the prompt objective must be clearly defined to ensure that the input structure aligns with the intended task. Second, understanding the model’s capabilities and limitations is essential to ensure compatibility between the prompt and the model’s generation abilities. Third, selecting an appro-priate prompt format is crucial, as well-structured and ex-plicit prompts consistently lead to higher-quality responses. Additionally, providing sufficient contextual information helps prevent generic outputs and reduces the risk of misinformation, particularly in less-represented or complex scenarios. Finally, prompts should be iteratively tested and refined according to the model’s responses to improve accuracy and reliability before deployment [51]. Prompting techniques are especially valuable for UAV appli-cations that require real-time decision-making and low-latency responses, such as trajectory optimization and resource alloca-tion. These techniques enable LLMs to interpret sensor data and environmental information directly, generating actionable outputs without extensive retraining. This avoids the significant computational overhead associated with conventional model training. For instance, ICL enables LLMs to perform new UAV-related tasks by providing example demonstrations, with performance strongly influenced by the design and organization of these examples. Similarly, CoT prompting promotes deeper reasoning by guiding the model through intermediate steps, enabling it to handle complex multi-stage UAV tasks such as dynamic obstacle avoidance, swarm coordination, and multi-agent decision-making. Prompt engineering is particularly well-suited to the con-straints of UAV networks for three main reasons. First, it is highly resource-efficient, requiring only a forward pass dur-ing inference and eliminating the computational and memory costs associated with backpropagation and gradient updates. This makes it suitable for deployment on resource-constrained network devices. Second, it offers high flexibility: by modifying textual prompts, a single LLM can be quickly adapted to a wide range of network tasks without additional coding or retraining. Finally, prompt engineering enables rapid implementation by leveraging the LLM’s inference capabilities to achieve low response times, a critical feature for low-latency services in dynamic UAV network environments. 1) In-Context Learning - ICL: ICL enables LLMs to adapt to target tasks by conditioning on natural-language instructions and demonstrations provided directly in the prompt, without modifying model parameters. In UASNets, ICL operates by providing contextualized demonstrations, such as prior network states, scheduling decisions, and environmental conditions, that allow the model to exploit pretrained knowledge for real-time adaptation. The effectiveness of ICL depends heavily on demonstration selection and formatting, requiring domain expertise in both UAV networking and LLM behavior. In practical UAV networking scenarios, such as data-collection scheduling, ICL enables systems to dynamically adapt to time-varying network conditions by incorporating real-time feedback into successive prompts. Sensory information, including queue lengths, channel conditions, and battery levels, can be converted into natural-language task descriptions and combined with historical decisions to guide the LLM. This approach is especially useful in emergency scenarios, such as Search and Rescue (SAR) missions, where conventional DRL-assisted methods require extensive training time and computa-tional resources [52]. 2) Chain-of-Thought Reasoning: CoT reasoning represents a significant advance in how AI systems handle complex reason-ing tasks by explicitly decomposing problems into intermediate steps [57]. Instead of generating answers directly, CoT prompts models to reason step by step, mirroring human problem-solving processes and improving performance on tasks in-volving mathematics, logic, planning, and multi-step decision-making. By revealing intermediate reasoning, CoT enhances interpretability and explainability, improves generalization, and helps mitigate hallucinations by constraining generation to verifiable logical sequences. Since its introduction, CoT prompting has evolved from basic paradigms such as Zero-shot CoT and Few-shot CoT to more advanced and scalable frameworks. These include Auto-CoT, which automatically selects effective demonstrations, and Self-Consistency CoT, which aggregates multiple reasoning paths to improve reliability. More recent extensions, such as Tree-of-Thought (ToT) and Graph-of-Thought (GoT), further structure reasoning as trees or graphs, enabling exploration and comparison of alternative solution trajectories. Although these advanced methods introduce additional computational overhead, they are particularly valuable for complex, intent-driven tasks, such as UAV networking, where transparent, logical, and verifiable decision-making is essential [58]. However, ICL and CoT prompting are primarily effective for single-task or narrowly scoped scenarios and may struggle with problems that require extensive information integration or long-horizon, multi-stage reasoning. Many UAV networking tasks involve interdependent subtasks, such as coordinated configuration, planning, and execution across multiple agents. Prompt-based planning addresses these limitations by explicitly generating and organizing structured subtask sequences, en-abling LLMs to tackle complex objectives more systematically and at scale. 

3) Prompt-Based Planning: For large-scale, complex tasks such as network configuration or project development, prompt-based planning breaks down the overall objective into a se-quence of manageable sub-tasks. In this approach, the LLM first generates a structured plan of ordered sub-tasks, which are then executed sequentially. This enables the model to address problems that require multi-step reasoning and the integration of information from multiple sources. Prompt-based planning further improves problem-solving by guiding the LLM through explicit action sequences, incorporating intermediate feedback, and continuously refining the plan. This capability is especially important for UAV operations, where tasks such as formation reconfiguration, resource allo-cation in multi-UAV systems, and coordinated mission plan-ning require long-horizon reasoning across multiple agents in dynamic environments. Therefore, designing effective prompts and planning structures is essential to the successful deploy-ment of LLMs in real-time UAV control and decision-making applications. Although prompt-based planning enables LLMs to decom-pose complex objectives into executable subtasks, the initial plans and decisions produced by the model may still be imperfect or suboptimal. Therefore, self-refinement is essential. 

4) Self-Refinement: Self-refinement is an emerging paradigm for improving the initial outputs through an iterative process of self-critique and refinement. In this approach, an LLM evaluates its own outputs and incrementally improves them based on internally generated feedback. For example, if an initial decision, such as a data collection schedule, is suboptimal or incorrect, the same model can be prompted to analyze its own output, identify deficiencies, and generate a revised solution. This significantly reduces the need for human intervention, which is particularly valuable given the complexity of networking and UAV-related tasks [50]. A salient merit of self-refinement is that it requires only a single LLM, which simultaneously serves as the generator, critic, and refiner, without relying on additional training data or RL. The process typically begins with the generation of an initial output, followed by a self-critique stage in which the model provides feedback on its own reasoning or results. This feedback is then used to generate an improved output, and the cycle is repeated until a predefined stopping criterion, such as convergence, task completion, or iteration limits, is met [60]. F. Key Findings and Insights 

The adaptation of LLMs to UAV systems requires carefully balancing performance, computational efficiency, and domain specificity. Pre-training offers broad generalization but remains economically and computationally infeasible for most UAV applications. Fine-tuning provides task specialization but de-pends on high-quality labeled data and introduces risks of overfitting. In contrast, prompt engineering and RAG emerge as lightweight and flexible approaches that enable task adaptation without parameter updates, making them particularly suitable for dynamic UAV environments. Prompt engineering shapes model behavior through struc-tured instructions and reasoning strategies such as ICL and CoT, enabling real-time decision support with minimal compu-tational overhead. RAG complements this by grounding gen-eration in external, up-to-date knowledge sources, improving factual accuracy in safety-critical UAV operations. While RAG increases system complexity through knowledge base mainte-nance and retrieval overhead, it reduces hallucination risks and supports continual adaptation without retraining. Importantly, these adaptation strategies should not be viewed as mutually exclusive. Fine-tuning is advantageous for stable, domain-specific behaviors, whereas RAG and prompt-based methods are better suited for rapidly evolving networking standards and mission contexts. Future LLM-assisted UAV systems will likely integrate these techniques within layered architectures, combining lightweight edge models for real-time control with larger models for strategic reasoning and mission planning. Despite these advantages, effective deployment remains chal-lenging due to constraints such as limited computational and memory resources on edge devices, latency and bandwidth restrictions, long-context processing requirements, reliability and safety concerns, data scarcity, and the dynamic nature of aerial networks. Addressing these challenges necessitates a combination of complementary deployment strategies, in-cluding centralized and on-device inference, collaborative and partitioned model architectures, and advances in memory man-agement, long-context handling, chunking mechanisms, and multimodal reasoning. The following provide a comprehensive discussion of these techniques, examining their trade-offs, recent advancements, and future research directions for scaling LLMs to meet the stringent operational demands of UAVs in mission-critical and highly dynamic environments. 

1) Edge, On-Device, and Hybrid LLM Deployment :

Thanks to their strong generalization and reasoning capa-bilities, LLMs enable UAVs to interpret complex and dy-namic environments, thereby supporting coherent control strate-gies for navigation, surveillance, and autonomous decision-making. These capabilities make LLMs particularly attractive for mission-critical, real-time applications such as SAR, Intel-ligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance (ISR), infrastructure inspection, and persistent aerial monitoring. However, most existing LLM deployments rely on cloud-based infrastructure, which has significant limitations in terms of latency, bandwidth consumption, and data privacy. Such constraints render cloud-centric approaches impractical, or even infeasible, for delay-sensitive or communication-constrained UAV operations. Inte-grating LLMs into UAV platforms has the potential to enhance onboard perception and autonomy significantly, but it also intro-duces substantial computational and communication challenges. Existing deployment strategies can be broadly categorized into four primary paradigms, as summarized in Table V. a) Centralized Edge Inference: In this approach, UAVs transmit sensor data to an edge server or ground station hosting the LLM for inference. Although this paradigm is communication-intensive, techniques such as token reduc-tion can help mitigate transmission overhead. Deploying LLMs at the edge typically requires model compression, quantization, pruning, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning to accommodate constrained hardware resources. When properly optimized, edge-based inference can support real-time decision-making, which is essential for safety-critical UAV operations. b) Local Inference (On-Device): Executing LLMs directly on UAV hardware improves data privacy, minimizes la-tency, and increases robustness against intermittent con-nectivity. Prior work, such as Aero-LLM [62], has demon-strated the feasibility of deploying very small models (e.g., OPT-125M/350M) on UAVs. However, such lightweight models often lack the reasoning depth required for com-plex tasks, including trajectory planning and resource allo-cation based on ground user requirements. On-device LLM deployment is further constrained by three key factors: (i) high computational demands, where generating even a single token may take several seconds on embedded platforms; (ii) excessive energy consumption, which can rapidly deplete UAV batteries; and (iii) stringent storage and memory limitations, as model parameters and inter-mediate activations frequently exceed the Random Access Memory (RAM) capacity of typical UAV systems [63]. c) Split Inference (Model Partitioning): In split inference, the LLM is partitioned such that the initial layers execute onboard the UAV to process raw sensor data. In contrast, deeper layers run on edge servers or cloud platforms. This strategy substantially reduces onboard computational requirements and limits data transmission to compact intermediate representations, rather than high-resolution raw sensor inputs. d) Collaborative Device–Server Inference: This paradigm leverages cooperation between lightweight onboard mod-els and powerful server-side LLMs. Techniques such as speculative decoding enable a small local model to gen-erate preliminary outputs, which are subsequently verified or refined by a larger remote model. Compared to trans-mitting raw multimodal data, this approach significantly reduces bandwidth requirements and is particularly well suited to environments with limited or unreliable commu-nication infrastructure [64]. While centralized edge inference can enable real-time LLM processing for UAV applications, its overall effectiveness is fun-damentally constrained by the memory capacity, computational resources, and energy availability of edge hardware. These limitations directly influence the scalability and practicality of deploying LLMs in UAV systems. Table V compares LLM inference paradigms for UAVs.

3) Scaling LLMs for Extended Contexts : LLMs struggle to process long contexts efficiently, which poses a fundamental limitation for real-world applications such as extended UAV missions and long-duration conversational interactions. This challenge stems from the architectural design of Transformer-based models, whose self-attention mechanism scales quadrat-ically with the context length, leading to both computational complexity and memory consumption. Consequently, process-ing long inputs becomes prohibitively expensive, often necessi-tating aggressive truncation that can degrade task performance. This limitation is further reinforced during pre-training, as most LLMs are trained on fixed-length context windows, inherently constraining their ability to maintain broader or persistent contextual understanding. Selective Context is an effective strategy to mitigate this issue by filtering out redundant or low-information content before the LLM processes it. By estimating token-level self-information, this approach identifies and dis-cards less informative tokens, thereby reducing memory usage and computational overhead while preserving the core seman-tic content. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that Selective Context can substantially reduce GPU memory consumption and accelerate text generation with only minimal performance degradation, enabling LLMs to process long documents and extended conversations without severe truncation [69]. A related and more structured approach, LLMLingua, ad-dresses the long-context challenge through hierarchical prompt compression. LLMLingua operates in two stages: first, a budget controller assigns different compression ratios to various com-ponents of the prompt (e.g., task instructions, demonstrations) to preserve semantic fidelity; second, a token-level iterative compressor further reduces the prompt by modeling conditional dependencies to retain critical information. Compared to Selec-tive Context, LLMLingua achieves stronger compression while better preserving semantic meaning [70]. Beyond prompt compression, architectural solutions can fur-ther alleviate long-context limitations by integrating explicit memory mechanisms. In particular, a conversation memory module can be composed of two complementary components: a) Short-Term Working Memory- A dynamic buffer that main-tains recent observations, actions, and intermediate states, en-suring local coherence and continuity; b) Long-Term Summary Memory- A compact and abstracted representation that stores key patterns, strategic insights, and accumulated knowledge over extended mission durations. In addition, Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)-assisted LLM designs can further enhance context retention and adaptive learning capabilities. Unlike conventional LLMs constrained by fixed context windows, AGI-inspired models incorporate episodic and semantic mem-ory mechanisms that dynamically store, retrieve, and update information over time. Such mechanisms reduce contextual drift and improve consistency and coherence in long-horizon inter-actions and decision-making processes [71]. Table VI compares techniques for scaling LLMs.

5) Quantization as an Inference Optimization Technique for Efficient LLMs : Quantization is a widely used model compression technique that improves the efficiency of LLMs by reducing the numerical precision of their parameters, and, in some cases, their activations, from high-precision floating-point formats (e.g., FP32 or FP16) to lower-bit representations such as INT8, INT4, or INT3. This reduction in precision substantially decreases model size, memory footprint, compu-tational complexity, inference latency, and energy consump-tion, making quantization particularly important for deploying LLMs on resource-constrained edge devices, including Rasp-berry Pi–class hardware. Quantization can be applied after training via Post-Training Quantization (PTQ), which converts pretrained model weights without retraining, or during training via Quantization-Aware Training (QAT), which simulates low-precision arithmetic to preserve accuracy better. Despite these efficiency gains, quantization introduces tradeoffs: aggressive reductions in bit-width may lead to accuracy degradation, numerical instability, or additional dequantization overhead, particularly for tasks involving complex reasoning or arith-metic. As a result, effective quantization requires carefully balancing efficiency gains with acceptable performance loss to enable practical, sustainable edge AI deployment. 

6) Distillation and Prunning : Distillation provides a prac-tical and scalable approach to compressing LLMs by enabling smaller student models to learn from the outputs and internal representations of a powerful teacher, rather than relying solely on task-specific supervision. It can be tightly integrated with techniques such as low-rank adaptation and supernet training, thereby requiring only a small number of additional parameters to train while keeping the pretrained weights frozen. This strat-egy enables efficient derivation of multiple model variants with different sizes, latencies, and accuracy tradeoffs from a single training run. As a result, distillation not only reduces model size and training cost but also supports dynamic deployment across heterogeneous edge devices, making it particularly well-suited to enterprise and edge AI scenarios where efficiency, flexibility, and performance must be carefully balanced. In parallel, LLM pruning further streamlines models by removing redundant or less important components, producing smaller, faster networks while preserving performance. III. LLM FOR UAV C OMMUNICATIONS 

Due to their high mobility and flexible deployment, UAVs have been widely adopted in civil and commercial applications such as environmental monitoring, traffic management, parcel delivery, and precision agriculture [72]–[75]. Beyond sensing and service roles, UAVs increasingly function as airborne com-munication relays for ground sensors in infrastructure-limited or emergency scenarios, including disaster response and border surveillance. By dynamically repositioning to establish short-range line-of-sight (LoS) links, UAVs enable reliable and high-throughput data transmission while extending network coverage beyond fixed terrestrial infrastructure. These capabilities make UAV-assisted networks particularly suitable for remote or haz-ardous environments, where rapid data collection and wide-area coverage are required with minimal human intervention [76]. LLMs are well suited to UAV communication systems because they directly address key challenges such as dy-namic network topology, heterogeneous information, and time-sensitive decision-making. Unlike conventional optimization or reinforcement learning approaches that rely on precise model-ing or extensive retraining, LLMs can reason over high-level task descriptions, historical solutions, and real-time feedback to generate communication-aware decisions with low iteration overhead. This section reviews recent efforts to integrate LLMs into UAV navigation, swarm coordination, safety, and network optimization, highlighting their emerging role in intelligent aerial communication systems. 

A. LLM-Assisted UAV Navigation, Planning, and Placement 

UAV navigation and mission-level planning are fundamental to autonomous SAR and ISR operations, as they determine where UAVs should move, how coverage should be allocated, and how multiple aerial assets should be positioned to ac-complish mission objectives. In complex and dynamic environ-ments, these planning decisions must account for environmental constraints, mission priorities, communication limitations, and regulatory requirements, often under strict time constraints. Traditional approaches to UAV navigation and multi-UAV planning, such as combinatorial optimization and RL, typically involve high computational costs and long convergence times, limiting their practicality for real-time or large-scale deploy-ment. Other learning-based methods, such as Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) and heuristic algorithms, often lack inter-pretability and struggle to adapt to rapidly changing mission contexts. Recent advances in LLMs provide a new paradigm for addressing these challenges by enabling high-level reasoning, semantic understanding, and iterative refinement of planning decisions using natural-language task descriptions, historical solutions, and performance feedback. In this subsection, we focus on the use of LLMs at the planning and decision-making layer, including UAV navigation, swarm-level path planning, and multi-UAV placement optimiza-tion. Rather than addressing low-level control or execution, the approaches discussed use LLMs to generate, evaluate, and refine high-level plans that guide downstream motion-planning and control modules. This perspective highlights the role of LLMs as cognitive planners that complement traditional opti-mization and learning techniques in complex UAV missions. Clader et al. [77] present a novel air–ground robotic sys-tem in which a UAV and an Unmanned Ground Vehicle (UGV) collaborate to execute missions specified in natural language within unknown, kilometer-scale environments. Their system employs an LLM-assisted planner, SPINE, to inter-pret high-level user intent, infer relevant semantic objects, and dynamically generate mission plans. A salient innovation is the construction of a compact, open-set semantic–metric map, built online by the UAV and opportunistically shared with the UGV via the MOCHA communication framework. This design enables effective coordination despite intermittent connectivity. Experimental results in both urban and rural environments demonstrate the system’s ability to complete seven distinct language-specified missions, such as inspecting a blocked bridge or triaging a chemical spill, highlighting its robustness in large-scale, unstructured settings. While this work focuses on air–ground cooperation for single-mission execution, subsequent studies have explored how LLM-assisted reasoning can scale to more complex multi-agent aerial systems. In this direction, Xiao et al. [78] propose an LLM-assisted framework for UAV swarm path planning in Industry 5.0 scenarios, such as low-altitude rescue missions. The approach addresses the limitations of traditional DNNs and heuristic algorithms in terms of interpretability and adaptability to dy-namic environments. The core of the method is the Multi-step Deep Thinking Movement Decision Samples Generation (MSDTMD-SG) algorithm, which creates fine-tuning datasets by simulating human-like multi-step reasoning. Using symbolic modeling and a DRL-inspired reward function, the algorithm identifies optimal movement positions from local observations and embeds the reasoning process into structured templates for LLM training. A cloud–edge collaboration framework enables UAVs to use a cloud-based large LLM when communication is stable and a compressed onboard small LLM when con-nectivity is limited. Additionally, a scene memory and replay mechanism supports continual learning from failures. Experi-mental evaluations show that the proposed method achieves the highest task completion rate compared to DRL and Ant Colony Optimization (ACO) baselines, while maintaining competitive computational cost and system latency, and providing superior adaptability in dynamic industrial environments. Beyond trajectory and path planning, researchers have ex-plored LLM-assisted approaches for high-level multi-UAV decision-making, focusing on placement and coordination rather than low-level swarm control. Wang et al. [79] introduce an LLM-assisted framework for optimizing the placement of multiple UAVs to establish Integrated Access and Backhaul (IAB) networks. Instead of relying on computationally inten-sive combinatorial optimization or RL methods, the proposed approach employs iterative structured prompting, enabling the LLM to generate and refine candidate UAV positions using Fig. 5: Diagram of the LLM-assisted UAV navigation, planning, and placement framework, illustrating the integration of mission context, reasoning, decision-making, and multi-agent coordination for autonomous operations.

Collectively, these studies demonstrate the versatility of LLMs in enhancing UAV capabilities across a broad spectrum, from single-robot air–ground coordination to swarm-level rea-soning and large-scale placement optimization. However, most existing work focuses on isolated components of the autonomy pipeline rather than providing a unified end-to-end solution. This limitation motivates broader efforts to integrate natural-language task interpretation with the full execution stack. Addressing this gap, Yuan et al. Through three representa-tive use cases, the framework demonstrates how LLMs can significantly reduce pilot workload while adhering to regula-tory, airspace, and environmental constraints. Furthermore, the authors propose a five-level autonomy roadmap that outlines the progression from current language-based instruction parsing to fully autonomous LLM-as-Autopilot systems, identifying critical challenges related to regulation-aware reasoning, data scarcity, and safety-critical evaluation. However, extending these capabilities to large-scale UAV swarms introduces additional coordination and control challenges. Using LLMs for intelligent, scalable, and context-aware swarm coordination offers a compelling approach to addressing these challenges and advancing the next generation of autonomous UAV systems.

D. Multi-LLM Architectures for UAV Communications 

Single LLM agents are often unreliable for safety-critical systems and can generate biased or unsafe outputs. In UAV swarm operations, where real-time decisions have significant safety implications, relying on a single model increases the risk of errors or unsafe behavior. To address these risks, multiple LLMs can be used to improve reliability, reduce bias, and ensure decisions comply with operational constraints and safety regulations. Multi-LLM systems leverage the complementary strengths of individual models, allowing for specialization, scalability, and robustness. By employing task decomposition and cross-verification, these systems enhance overall decision quality and resilience, thereby making them more suitable for safety-critical UAV swarm applications. Multi-LLM systems are architectures in which multiple LLMs collaborate, either in parallel or sequentially, to solve complex tasks. Unlike single-model approaches, multi-LLM systems leverage the complementary strengths of individual models, enabling specialization, scalability, robustness, and effective task decomposition. Performance is further improved through cross-verification, which helps detect errors, reduce bias, and mitigate hallucinations. Collaboration among LLMs can follow cooperative, competitive, or ensemble paradigms, while system architectures may be centralized or decentralized, depending on fault tolerance and task allocation requirements. Additionally, multi-LLM frameworks can enhance privacy by routing sensitive queries to trusted models while validating outputs across agents. Deployment strategies differ significantly between cloud and edge environments. Edge deployments offer low latency and improved privacy by processing data locally, but are constrained by limited computational and energy resources. Therefore, tech-niques such as model compression, partitioning, and distributed inference are often necessary. In contrast, cloud deployments provide abundant computational capacity and support large-scale reasoning, but may experience higher latency and be less suitable for real-time or privacy-sensitive UAV operations. In aerial and UAV networks, multi-LLM systems enable distributed coordination among heterogeneous agents, including delivery UAVs, urban air mobility vehicles, and UAV-assisted wireless nodes. Individual LLMs can specialize in tasks such as route planning, communication optimization, or mission-level decision-making, while sharing critical information on navigation, sensing, and system status. This division of labor improves safety, efficiency, and resilience by supporting real-time adaptation, collision avoidance, fault tolerance, and col-lective optimization – capabilities that are difficult to achieve with a single LLM or a fully centralized controller [91]. Building on this paradigm, Cui et al. [92] propose a UAV swarm inspection system in which multiple lightweight LLM sub-models operate in parallel across a UAV fleet. Local LLMs handle onboard path planning and real-time decision-making, while a centralized orchestrator performs global reasoning, task allocation, and swarm-level optimization. By fusing multimodal data, including maps, telemetry, and visual and thermal im-agery, the system dynamically adapts inspection paths and detects defects with high accuracy. Experimental results show improvements in coverage, energy efficiency, and robustness compared to traditional planning methods. Complementing this approach, Liu et al. [93] introduce a multi-agent LLM framework tailored to UAV-based inspection in the Architecture, Engineering, and Construction (AEC) do-main. Their system comprises specialized agents, including Router, PathPlanner, Controller, Perceptioner, and Retriever, that collectively support spatial reasoning, UAV control, and task delegation. A novel image-driven pipeline converts in-spection data into semantic point clouds and three-dimensional scene graphs, enabling UAVs to interpret environments in align-ment with human intent. Experiments demonstrate improved flexibility and automation in inspection workflows. Similarly, emphasizing distributed intelligence, Dharma-lingam et al. [62] present Aero-LLM, a hierarchical LLM architecture spanning the onboard, edge, and cloud layers. Each layer hosts specialized, fine-tuned models responsible for tasks such as anomaly detection [94], forecasting, and cybersecurity. This hierarchical decomposition enables low-latency respon-siveness while supporting high-level analytical reasoning, all within lightweight computational footprints. Evaluations high-light Aero-LLM’s effectiveness in threat detection and secure decision-making on resource-constrained UAV platforms. 4) LLM-assisted ISAC Optimization : The integration of LLMs into the optimization of Integrated Sensing and Commu-nication (ISAC) systems represents a significant methodological advance, as demonstrated by two recent studies that employ LLMs in complementary roles in complex wireless network design. Li et al. [98] examine a multi-UAV ISAC network in which UAVs must jointly provide communication services and radar-based localization of ground users. In this study, an LLM (GPT-3.5 Turbo) is incorporated as an intelligent search opera-tor within a Multi-Objective Evolutionary Algorithm (MOEA). By prompting the LLM with the problem context and current solution states, the model generates novel candidate solutions for UAV deployment and power allocation. This LLM-assisted search enables the evolutionary algorithm to more effectively explore the non-convex trade-off between network utility and sensing accuracy, resulting in improved Pareto fronts and faster convergence compared to conventional approaches. Complementarily, Li et al. [99] study a multi–base station MIMO ISAC system and address the joint optimization of discrete user association and continuous transmit beamforming. In this framework, the LLM (GPT-o1) serves as a combinatorial solver for the NP-hard user-association subproblem within an alternating-optimization loop. Through carefully engineered prompts incorporating ICL, CoT reasoning, and iterative self-reflection, the LLM infers high-quality association strategies. These results are then combined with rigorous convex op-timization techniques—such as fractional programming and the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), for beamforming design, yielding a hybrid solution that achieves near-optimal performance, closely matches brute-force upper bounds, and outperforms conventional game-theoretic methods. Overall, these studies highlight a paradigm shift in ISAC op-timization, moving beyond purely model-based or data-driven techniques. Instead of serving as standalone solvers, LLMs function as powerful complementary components that enhance traditional optimization frameworks. Their main strength is in addressing problem segments with combinatorial complexity, non-convexity, or a lack of explicit gradient information, where implicit trade-off reasoning is crucial. By encoding domain knowledge and constraints via natural-language prompting, LLMs provide a flexible reasoning interface that reduces re-liance on extensive mathematical reformulation and scenario-specific expert tuning. 

5) High-Speed Connectivity for LLM-assisted UAVs : The effectiveness of LLM-assisted reasoning in UAV systems de-pends critically on the reliability, latency, and timeliness of the underlying communication links. While LLMs extend UAV capabilities beyond automation by supporting summarization, semantic understanding, and adaptive mission support, these functions rely on continuous, high-quality data exchange. UAV-assisted URLLC therefore serves as a foundational layer that enables closed-loop perception, real-time human–machine in-teraction, and dependable reasoning in mission-critical sce-narios such as disaster response, search and rescue, and in-frastructure inspection. Without ultra-reliable and low-latency communication, the system-level advantages of LLM-assisted intelligence cannot be sustained in real-world deployments. 5G networks, leveraging key technologies such as Millimeter-Wave (mmWave) communication, massive MIMO, and advanced beamforming, enable URLLC and are well-suited for mission-critical applications such as UAV-assisted SAR. URLLC can achieve end-to-end latencies of 1 ms or less, supporting real-time decision-making in highly time-sensitive scenarios. The resulting high-throughput, low-latency communication links between UAVs and edge infrastructure allow rapid transmission of multimodal sensor data for immediate processing. This capability enables on-the-fly LLM inference, allowing UAVs to make responsive, context-aware decisions in dynamic, uncertain environments. By offloading computationally intensive tasks to edge servers, 5G connectivity effectively overcomes many of the resource constraints inherent to onboard UAV platforms. Nevertheless, despite the advantages of high-speed connec-tivity, UAV-assisted LLM systems still face intrinsic challenges when handling long data sequences. Transformer-based LLMs have quadratic computational and memory complexity with respect to input sequence length, making long-context pro-cessing increasingly expensive even when inference is partially offloaded. As a result, extended missions, continuous perception streams, and prolonged human–UAV interactions remain diffi-cult to support efficiently. Therefore, addressing long-context modeling and efficient attention mechanisms is essential to fully exploit LLMs’ real-time, 5G-assisted capabilities in dynamic UAV operations. 

6) Assessing LLM Reasoning and Safety : Field deploy-ments of integrated LLM-enabled UAV systems confirm sig-nificant improvements in operational adaptability and effective-ness across diverse applications, including emergency response, environmental monitoring, and infrastructure inspection. At the same time, these evaluations expose persistent constraints related to bandwidth availability, energy consumption, multi-modal robustness, and reasoning reliability. These observations highlight the necessity of holistic co-design across communi-cation, computation, and control, as well as the importance of standardized evaluation frameworks, such as UAVBench and UAVThreatBench, to objectively assess reasoning performance and cybersecurity readiness for safe and scalable deployment of intelligent UAV communication systems. UAVBench marks a significant advancement for the au-tonomous aerial systems community by providing the first large-scale, standardized, and physically grounded framework for evaluating LLM reasoning in realistic UAV mission sce-narios. Prior to UAVBench, no unified methodology existed to assess whether LLMs genuinely understood the critical constraints governing aerial autonomy, such as aerodynamics, flight safety, navigation limits, airspace structure, and ethical decision-making. This absence of standardized evaluation made it difficult to distinguish models capable of supporting trust-worthy UAV operations from those that generate superficially plausible but unreliable responses. The benchmark introduced in [100] addresses this gap by providing 50,000 validated UAV scenarios and an additional 50,000 multiple-choice questions ( U AV Bench M CQ ) spanning 10 distinct reasoning styles. This comprehensive design enables systematic evaluation of a wide range of cognitive capabili-ties, from basic situational awareness to advanced multi-agent coordination and ethics-aware decision-making under opera-tional constraints. Empirical evaluation of 32 state-of-the-art LLMs reveals that, while current models perform relatively well on perception-related and policy-level reasoning tasks, they continue to struggle with ethical judgment, resource-constrained planning, and the complex trade-offs inherent in real-world aerial missions. By releasing both the benchmark and its supporting tools, UAVBench enables the community to diagnose model limitations, compare approaches on a standard foundation, and guide the development of next-generation UAV intelligence through rigorous, transparent evaluation. Complementing UAVBench, UAVThreatBench introduces a structured benchmark for evaluating LLM performance in UAV cybersecurity within industrial operational technology environ-ments, in accordance with the European Radio Equipment Directive (RED). The benchmark comprises 924 expert-curated scenarios, each annotated with five distinct threats, yielding 4,620 RED-mapped threats across network and device integrity, privacy protection, and fraud prevention. Seven state-of-the-art LLMs, including OpenAI’s GPT-4 variants and LLaMA-3 mod-els, were evaluated on 100 scenarios using fuzzy matching to assess alignment with expert-defined threats. Instruction-tuned models, such as GPT-4o, achieved the highest performance, reaching nearly 87% total threat matching, while open-weight models showed lower alignment and particular difficulty with availability and backend-layer threats. Further semantic analysis revealed systematic gaps in detecting denial-of-service vectors and vulnerabilities in MES/WMS logic. Together, UAVBench and UAVThreatBench provide com-plementary, standardized evaluation frameworks that address both operational reasoning and cybersecurity resilience. By offering objective, mission-relevant benchmarks, these contri-butions equip the UAV research community with tools to assess, compare, and improve LLM-assisted autonomy, paving the way for more reliable, responsible, and deployment-ready intelligent aerial systems. REASONING IN UAV O PERATIONS AND COMMUNICATIONS 

This section examines the emergence of MLLMs as a uni-fying intelligence paradigm that integrates visual, linguistic, and sensor information for UAV systems. By extending large language models with multimodal perception and reasoning capabilities, MLLMs enable UAVs to interpret complex en-vironments, understand high-level human intent, and support context-aware autonomous decision-making. Recent advances in MLLMs have demonstrated that jointly reasoning over language, vision, and heterogeneous sensor inputs is critical for aerial platforms operating in dynamic, partially observable environments. In UAV applications, this multimodal integration enables intuitive human–machine inter-action, robust perception and navigation, and adaptive mission execution by grounding abstract instructions in real-time sen-sory observations. Compared with text-only models, MLLMs provide a more natural interface between human intent and autonomous aerial behavior, particularly in scenarios involving VLN, swarm coordination, and mission-level planning. Building on this foundation, this section reviews the core architectural principles of MLLMs, practical training and adaptation strategies for aerial data, and emerging multi-modal reasoning mechanisms such as Multimodal Chain-of-Thought (MCoT) and Multimodal In-Context Learning (M-ICL). We then survey representative UAV applications enabled by MLLMs, including human–swarm interaction, VLN, au-tonomous mission planning, and real-time operational intel-ligence. Finally, we discuss key challenges related to safety, efficiency, and reliability that must be addressed to enable robust deployment of multimodal intelligence on resource-constrained aerial platforms. 

A. Building and Fine-Tuning Multimodal LLMs 

Training MLLMs for UAV applications begins with con-structing multimodal datasets that reflect the unique sensing conditions of aerial platforms. Typical data sources include aerial images and videos, onboard sensor measurements, and associated textual descriptions or operational logs. Compared with ground-level imagery, UAV data exhibit distinctive view-points, scale variations, and occlusions, making UAV-specific datasets essential for effective multimodal alignment. Conse-quently, data augmentation and domain-aware preprocessing are commonly employed to improve robustness under diverse flight conditions. Most MLLMs are initialized through large-scale self-supervised multimodal pretraining to acquire general cross-modal representations. While techniques such as contrastive learning are widely adopted, their primary role in UAV appli-cations is to provide transferable multimodal priors rather than task-specific aerial knowledge. As a result, pretrained models often require substantial adaptation to handle the geometric and semantic characteristics of aerial scenes. A key challenge lies in aligning heterogeneous modalities for grounded reasoning. Contemporary MLLMs typically rely on modality-specific encoders coupled with a pretrained LLM, enabling language modules to attend selectively to visual fea-tures. This flexible architecture serves as a common backbone for UAV perception and reasoning tasks, but its effectiveness in aerial settings depends strongly on downstream adaptation. Following pretraining, models are fine-tuned on UAV-specific tasks such as aerial image captioning, visual question an-swering, and object recognition. Dedicated datasets including AeroCaps [101] and HRVQA-VL [102] support fine-grained scene understanding and multi-hop reasoning over UAV im-agery. Empirical evidence shows that task-aware fine-tuning is critical: compact MLLMs (e.g., 2B parameters) adapted with UAV data can outperform significantly larger generic models while requiring only a few gigabytes of FP16 memory. In practice, training large multimodal models from scratch is rarely feasible for UAV systems. Instead, transfer learning and parameter-efficient adaptation techniques are widely adopted. By freezing pretrained backbones and updating lightweight modules such as low-rank adapters, MLLMs can be efficiently aligned with aerial semantics at reduced computational and memory cost, making them suitable for edge-deployed and resource-constrained UAV platforms [102]. Overall, effective MLLM deployment in UAV applications relies less on generic multimodal pretraining strategies and more on UAV-specific data, efficient adaptation, and careful alignment with operational constraints. 

B. MCoT and M-ICL: Foundations of Multimodal Reasoning 

MCoT reasoning extends the traditional CoT paradigm, origi-nally developed for text-based LLMs, to multimodal settings by incorporating diverse data modalities, including images, videos, audio, speech, 3D data, tables, and charts. At its core, MCoT decomposes complex multimodal tasks into a sequence of inter-mediate reasoning steps, called thoughts, that together form a structured rationale before producing the final answer. Formally, given a multimodal input composed of language tokens T

and non-linguistic modalities M, MCoT can be categorized into two scenarios based on the nature of the rationale R. In Scenario 1, the model generates text-only rationales to reason over multimodal inputs and outputs. In Scenario 2, the rationale itself incorporates multimodal elements, either as purely non-textual signals or as a hybrid of textual and multimodal con-tent. By explicitly modeling the step-by-step progression from perception to cognition, this structured reasoning paradigm enhances interpretability, reduces hallucination, and improves performance on complex multimodal tasks. The development of MCoT is closely linked to recent ad-vances in multimodal LLMs such as GPT-4V, Gemini, and Claude3, which combine multimodal encoders with autoregres-sive decoders to process and generate cross-modal content. Despite this progress, several challenges remain, including the computational sustainability of long reasoning chains, er-ror accumulation and propagation across intermediate steps, modality imbalance, hallucination mitigation, and the need for explainable and ethically aligned reasoning frameworks. Future research directions emphasize hybrid neuro-symbolic integration, adaptive reasoning in dynamic environments, and interdisciplinary insights from cognitive science, to advance MCoT toward more general and robust multimodal AGI [103]. M-ICL is another important emergent capability of large mul-timodal models. By presenting a small number of multimodal examples directly in the prompt, M-ICL enables models to solve novel tasks by analogy, significantly reducing reliance on large labeled datasets. Its training-free nature allows seamless deployment across a wide range of inference-time scenarios. Formally, M-ICL is defined as an inference-time adaptation mechanism for Large Multimodal Models (LMMs), in which the model processes a context C consisting of N multimodal demonstration tuples (Ii, T i, R i). Here, Ii denotes an image, 

Ti represents optional associated text (such as a question or instruction), and Ri is the corresponding target response. This context is accompanied by a query Q = ( IQ, T Q), and the model generates an output O = LMM (C, Q ) under a causal language modeling objective, without any updates of gradient-based parameters. 

C. MLLM-Assisted Human–Swarm Interaction and Autonomy 

Integrating MLLMs into UAV systems enables UAVs to more effectively synthesize information from multiple sensors. In the following, we examine how MLLMs can enhance the performance and scalability of UAV systems, focusing on their potential to improve multi-agent perception, navigation, and coordination within UAV swarms, as well as their role in ensuring secure and resilient swarm operations. Recent advances in MLLMs have significantly expanded the capabilities of Human–Swarm Interaction (HSI) and au-tonomous UAV systems. Eunmi et al. [104] highlight this shift by introducing SwarmChat, a context-aware interaction framework in which LLM-assisted modules, context genera-tion, intent recognition, task planning, and modality selection collaborate to interpret user commands and adapt them to real-time swarm states. Their results demonstrate that natural-language, voice, and teleoperation interfaces can substantially reduce cognitive load. However, the system’s rule-based intent recognition and limited experimental scope indicate room for more scalable, learning-based solutions. Building on the idea that LLMs can mediate complex swarm behaviors, Liu et al. [105] present a multimodal GPT-4–based approach for UAV formation control using both visual and textual inputs. Their results show that MLLMs can extract commands from human instructions, interpret environmental cues from onboard imagery, and plan formations with over 80% success. This work validates the feasibility of end-to-end multimodal reasoning for swarm perception, coordination, and adaptive task execution, complementing the interaction-focused perspective introduced by SwarmChat. Extending multimodal reasoning into broader autonomous decision-making, Ping et al. [64] explore how MLLMs can enhance multi-agent perception, navigation, and coordination within UAV swarms. Through a forest-firefighting case study, they demonstrate that an MLLM (Doubao 1.5 Vision Pro) can perform natural-language task planning, dynamic fire assessment, and coordinated execution across multiple UAVs. Their discussion of challenges, such as hallucination, inference latency, and communication bottlenecks, further contextualizes the limitations identified in earlier work and underscores the need for more efficient, robust multimodal pipelines. In addition to these operational advances, Xiao et al. [106] examine the role of MLLMs in secure and resilient swarm operations and propose a multimodal intrusion-detection archi-tecture that integrates cyber and physical data streams. Their hierarchical zero-trust framework distributes different model sizes across edge, fog, and command layers, optimizing infer-ence latency and computational cost while enhancing detection robustness. This security-focused perspective underscores the need for trustworthy MLLM integration as swarms become in-creasingly autonomous and data-intensive. From a control inter-face standpoint, Balaji et al. [107] introduce PromptPilot, which uses natural language instructions to control UAVs without requiring domain expertise. By combining a lightweight intent classifier with MLLMs such as Gemini or LLaVA, along with YOLOv10 and DeepSORT for vision-aware tasks, PromptPilot generates executable control code and demonstrates reliable autonomous performance. This work aligns with SwarmChat and Liu et al. in showing that intuitive human–UAV interaction can be achieved through MLLMs, while emphasizing practical deployment and real-world testing. Finally, Yang et al. [108] focus on rapid multimodal situational reporting, integrating UAV imagery, ground sensor data, and meteorological in-formation to generate comprehensive forest-fire intelligence. Their system fuses Grounding DINO, YOLOv8-based models, and the DeepSeek LLM to identify critical environmental features and compute safe evacuation routes via enhanced A. This application illustrates how multimodal perception and language-based reasoning can support high-stakes environmen-tal decision-making, complementing the swarm-control and interaction frameworks previously discussed. Eval-uated across diverse simulation environments without task-specific fine-tuning, VLFly consistently outperforms previous approaches. H. Key Findings and Insights 

This section consolidates key observations and empirical findings on the deployment of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) in UAV applications. Emphasis is placed on quantitative evidence, benchmark results, and UAV-specific limitations that directly affect real-world reliability, efficiency, and safety as sequel. 

1) Benchmarking MLLMs for Multi-UAV Collaborative Perception : AirCopBench [123] is the first benchmark explic-itly designed to evaluate MLLMs in multi-UAV collaborative perception under degraded sensing conditions. The benchmark contains over 2,900 multi-view aerial images and more than 14,600 VQA samples, covering four task categories (Scene Understanding, Object Understanding, Perception Assessment, and Collaborative Decision) across 14 task types. Evaluation of 40 state-of-the-art MLLMs reveals a substantial performance gap: the best-performing model achieves only 59.17% accuracy, lagging human performance by more than 24% on average, with large variance across task categories. Fine-tuning results indicate that sim-to-real transfer can partially mitigate these gaps, highlighting the importance of UAV-specific data and training strategies for collaborative aerial perception. 

2) Speculative Decoding for Efficient LLM Inference :

Inference latency remains a critical bottleneck for UAV de-ployment, as conventional autoregressive decoding requires one forward pass per generated token. Speculative decoding improves efficiency by allowing multiple candidate tokens to be generated and verified in parallel within a single forward pass, significantly reducing end-to-end latency while preserving output correctness. Recent approaches such as EAGLE-3 fur-ther eliminate the need for a separate draft model by exploiting internal hidden states of the target model. Although primarily evaluated in general-purpose LLM settings, these techniques are highly relevant to UAV platforms, where real-time respon-siveness and energy efficiency are tightly constrained. 

3) Long-Context Modeling in Multimodal Systems : Long-context modeling enables multimodal systems to reason over extended inputs, but scaling context length introduces chal-lenges including quadratic attention complexity, positional en-coding degradation, and mid-context retrieval failure. Recent solutions—such as efficient attention mechanisms, memory-augmented architectures, and chunked processing—improve scalability, while data-centric strategies (e.g., synthetic long-context generation and short-to-long alignment) enhance ro-bustness. However, for UAV applications involving long-duration missions or multi-stage reasoning, a gap persists between theoretical context length and effective usable context, limiting practical long-horizon multimodal reasoning [124]. 

4) Multi-Agent Framework for MLLMs Training : The high computational cost of end-to-end multimodal training motivates modular and multi-agent alternatives. BEMYEYES [125] adopts a collaborative framework in which a lightweight visual “perceiver” extracts scene information and a powerful text-only LLM acts as a “reasoner.” Through multi-turn interac-tion, this system enables multimodal reasoning without training large-scale vision–language models. Experimental results show that this fully open-source framework can match or surpass proprietary models such as GPT-4o on several knowledge-intensive tasks, demonstrating a scalable and resource-efficient path for multimodal reasoning in UAV systems. 

5) Addressing Visual Hallucination in Multimodal LLMs :

MLLMs have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks that integrate text and visual information. Despite these advances, a persistent challenge is Visual Hal-lucination (VH), the generation of outputs containing details inconsistent with the visual input. In other words, MLLMs may ”imagine” objects, attributes, or relationships that are absent from an image, particularly in tasks such as object recognition. This phenomenon undermines model reliability and limits their safe and effective deployment in UAV applications. 

6) Balancing Efficiency and Reasoning Diversity in Vision-Language Models for UAV Applications : Results in [102] demonstrate that UAV-oriented vision–language models can significantly outperform general-purpose models when adapted with domain-specific data and training strategies. The proposed UAV-VL-R1 model achieves strong reasoning performance while maintaining a compact footprint suitable for onboard de-ployment. Ablation studies reveal a key trade-off: while super-vised fine-tuning (SFT) accelerates convergence and stabilizes early performance, excessive reliance on SFT reduces reason-ing diversity, particularly in counting and numerical inference tasks. In contrast, the multi-stage GRPO framework enhances reasoning diversity and cross-task generalization through intra-group reward comparisons. The associated HRVQA-VL dataset, consisting of 50,019 samples organized into progressively chal-lenging stages, further highlights that domain-specific data can outweigh sheer model scale, outperforming both 2B and 72B Qwen2-VL baselines under UAV constraints. 

7) Understanding and Evaluating Hallucination Behavior in Aerial LVLMs : Basak et al. [101] introduce AeroCaps, the first aerial-view image captioning dataset, comprising 1,256 human-annotated images. Their analysis shows that aerial per-spectives significantly amplify hallucination rates, especially for object identity, spatial relations, and numerical descriptions, with error frequency increasing for longer captions. Notably, mitigation strategies such as object hints and LLM-based self-evaluation exhibit limited effectiveness, underscoring the difficulty of reliable hallucination detection. These findings indicate that current LVLMs remain insufficiently robust for real-world UAV deployment without further advances in dataset scale, evaluation rigor, and grounding-aware model design. The study emphasizes that, despite impressive performance on conventional benchmarks, current LVLMs are not yet suffi-ciently robust or trustworthy for deployment in real-world aerial applications, including automated surveillance, environmental monitoring, and disaster response. The results suggest that progress in this area will require not only improved model architectures and training strategies but also domain-specific datasets, rigorous evaluation protocols, and transparent report-ing of failure modes. As aerial data becomes increasingly cen-tral to AI-driven decision-making, addressing these challenges will be critical to ensuring the safe deployment of AI. In summary, based on Sections II and III, the multifaceted in-tegration of LLMs and MLLMs into autonomous aerial systems necessitates an investigation of their ethical implications. LLMs are becoming central to UAV decision-making, perception, and control – capabilities with significant real-world consequences. Section II describes how LLMs, through prompt engineering and RAG, enable UAVs to perform complex tasks such as disaster response and navigation autonomously. The shift to MLLMs in Section III further deepens this integration, allowing UAVs to fuse and interpret streams of visual, sensory, and linguistic data to navigate urban environments, manage swarms, and conduct mission-critical operations such as SAR. Addition-ally, the technical capabilities and inherent vulnerabilities of LLMs, as outlined in these sections, create specific and urgent ethical imperatives. For example, LLMs are prone to hallu-cinations, generating plausible yet nonfactual content, which raises concerns about their reliability. This flaw could lead to significant navigation errors or misidentification of targets in the field. Their black-box nature and sophisticated reasoning make it difficult to audit critical UAV decisions. This opacity is compounded in MLLMs, where adversarial attacks can exploit visual vulnerabilities to bypass safety protocols and potentially hijack UAV systems. Moreover, LLMs are trained on large-scale datasets that inevitably contain societal biases; if a model prioritizes disaster relief efforts or the identification of persons of interest, it could systematically disadvantage certain groups. Finally, there are environmental concerns regarding energy consumption and sustainability. Therefore, investigating the ethical implications is critical to the responsible development and deployment of LLM-assisted UAVs. This ensures that the pursuit of technological autonomy is guided by safeguards for safety, fairness, accountability, and societal trust, preventing harm and aligning these systems with human values and safety-critical standards. V. E THICAL CHALLENGES AND BEST PRACTICES FOR 

LLM I NTEGRATION IN UAV S

As LLMs and MLLMs become integral to autonomous UAV decision-making, their deployment introduces critical ethical and safety challenges that must be systematically addressed. Privacy risks arise when large-scale datasets include sensitive information collected without explicit consent. Bias and fair-ness issues may emerge from imbalanced training data, leading to unsafe or discriminatory UAV decisions. Accountability and transparency are further complicated by the black-box nature of LLMs, which hinders failure diagnosis and responsibility attribution. In addition, the high computational cost of LLM training and inference raises sustainability concerns, motivating energy-efficient and environmentally responsible AI practices. This section reviews representative ethical challenges in LAUS and surveys emerging best practices for secure, transpar-ent, and accountable deployment in real-world UAV scenarios. 

A. Securing UAV Operations: Privacy and Data Protection with LLMs 

While LLMs enhance autonomy and reasoning in UAV net-works, they also introduce security and privacy vulnerabilities, including prompt manipulation, leakage of sensitive sensor data, unauthorized access, and unsafe decision-making under adversarial conditions. When LLMs influence mission planning, resource scheduling, or control actions, such vulnerabilities can result in severe operational failures. LLM security encompasses practices that protect models, data, and outputs across devel-opment, deployment, and real-time operation. Core objectives include preventing unauthorized access, mitigating adversarial attacks such as jailbreaking and data poisoning, and ensuring compliance with safety and operational constraints. Jailbreaking is a particularly critical threat in LAUS. These attacks manipulate prompts to bypass safety constraints and induce unauthorized behavior. White-box attacks assume access to internal model information, enabling gradient- or logit-based manipulation, while black-box attacks rely solely on crafted prompts and are therefore more practical in real-world deployments. Among black-box methods, template completion attacks are especially dangerous. By embedding harmful in-tent within structured templates, fictional scenarios, or role-playing contexts, attackers exploit LLMs’ pattern-completion behavior while evading keyword-based filters. Context-based manipulation and code injection further amplify these risks, particularly for models relying heavily on contextual coherence and demonstration-based reasoning. These risks are amplified in UAV networks using ICL for real-time decision-making. For example, malicious demonstrations injected into scheduling prompts can bias UAVs toward suboptimal sensing actions, such as prioritizing sensors with poor channel conditions, lead-ing to increased packet loss, degraded network performance, and compromised mission objectives [52]. This illustrates how prompt-level attacks can propagate into system-level failures. Mitigation requires a multi-layered defense strategy. Inte-grating an independent safety verifier allows LLM-generated actions to be evaluated against predefined constraints and overridden if unsafe. Complementary attack detection modules can monitor prompt statistics, such as perplexity or structural anomalies, to identify manipulations in real time. Together, these mechanisms enable robust operation under adversarial conditions. 

B. Bias-Aware LLMs for Safer UAV Operations and Commu-nications 

Bias in LLMs arises from imbalanced or unrepresentative training data and can directly affect safety and mission effec-tiveness in UAV systems, for example through skewed threat assessment or unfair scheduling decisions. These risks are particularly severe in post-disaster scenarios characterized by uncertainty, limited communication, and strict latency con-straints. In large-scale SAR missions, relying on a single mono-lithic LLM for perception, planning, and scheduling can create an operational bottleneck. Under high cognitive load, such models are more prone to hallucinations, biased prioritization, and delayed responses, violating key requirements for multi-UAV operations, including real-time responsiveness, efficient resource use, and robustness to partial failures. To address these limitations, recent LLM-assisted UAV ar-chitectures distribute reasoning across ensembles of special-ized agents responsible for distinct tasks, such as trajectory planning, sensing scheduling, or risk assessment. Bias can be further mitigated by supervisory LLMs that review decisions for fairness, consistency, and compliance before execution [126]. This decentralized design improves situational awareness, re-duces redundant communication, and enhances system integrity through cross-agent verification. Beyond architecture, LLM observability plays a critical role in bias mitigation by enabling inspection of intermediate rea-soning steps and decision patterns in real time [127]. While observability does not eliminate bias at its source, it provides a practical mechanism for detecting and correcting biased behavior in deployed systems. Complementing observability, Morales et al. [128] propose LangBiTe, a model-driven testing framework that enables con-tinuous ethical assessment through domain-specific specifi-cations and automated scenario generation. Such structured testing provides additional assurance for bias detection and accountability in LAUS. 

C. Ethical LLMs in Action: Ensuring Accountability and Trans-parency for UAV Operation and Communication

LLMs often function as opaque black boxes, making it difficult to interpret decisions, trace failures, or ensure consistent behavior. Enhancing causal reasoning offers a promis-ing pathway to improved interpretability. By modeling cause-and-effect relationships rather than relying solely on pattern recognition, LLMs can provide more transparent and auditable decision logic, enabling error diagnosis and accountability in complex, real-world UAV tasks. D. Sustainable Intelligence: Environmental Concerns in LLMs 

The environmental footprint of LLMs presents a growing eth-ical challenge that requires a lifecycle perspective encompass-ing training, inference, deployment, and hardware production. From an energy perspective, training dominates carbon emis-sions, with large-scale models consuming electricity compara-ble to hundreds of households, while inference incurs smaller per-query costs. However, at the scale of billions of daily interactions, inference energy consumption becomes a signifi-cant contributor to global emissions. Environmental impacts are further amplified by lifecycle factors such as resource-intensive hardware manufacturing, embodied carbon in AI accelerators, and regional variation in data-center energy mixes. Design trade-offs also matter: larger models may be more efficient per high-quality output, whereas smaller models can be preferable under high request volumes. Importantly, sustainable trajectories remain achievable through mitigation strategies including model quantization, energy-efficient accelerators, deployment on low-carbon infras-tructure, and standardized lifecycle environmental reporting. Aligning model design, infrastructure, and governance with transparency and renewable energy use is essential for reducing long-term ecological impact. In summary, while per-query emissions remain modest, the aggregate and systemic environ-mental impacts of LLMs are substantial. Achieving sustainable intelligence requires coordinated optimization across compu-tation, energy sourcing, hardware lifecycles, and transparent environmental governance [129], [130]. 

E. Key Findings and Insights 

Recent studies consistently show that effective integration of LLMs into UAV systems depends not only on model capabilities but also on system-level mechanisms that ensure reliability, safety, and accountability. In practice, this calls for a layered governance framework combining LLM observability, explicit safety verification, and HITL oversight. LLM observability forms the foundation of trustworthy de-ployment by linking output quality with accountability. Un-like traditional monitoring focused on latency or resource usage, observability enables continuous assessment of LLM outputs across correctness, relevance, coherence, and factual consistency, allowing early detection of model drift, halluci-nations, and performance degradation in dynamic UAV envi-ronments [131]. Modern observability extends classical logs and traces with LLM-specific signals, including structured prompt logging, user feedback, request tracing across multi-stage pipelines, and token-usage monitoring, supporting sys-tematic diagnosis and scalable optimization [132], [133]. However, observability alone is insufficient to guarantee safety. Due to their probabilistic nature, LLMs may still pro-duce decisions that violate operational or safety constraints. A second key insight is therefore the necessity of an independent safety verification layer, where LLM-generated decisions are evaluated against predefined rules and feasibility constraints and overridden when unsafe. This separation of adaptive rea-soning and deterministic enforcement enables UAV systems to combine flexibility with the reliability required for safety-critical operation. Representative observability and verification components are summarized in Table XIII. Beyond automated safeguards, HITL oversight remains indis-pensable. While LLMs provide strong reasoning and decision-support capabilities, they are inherently limited in handling ethical dilemmas, rare edge cases, and ambiguous real-world scenarios. HITL frameworks integrate human judgment into mission planning and real-time operation, allowing operators to intervene when uncertainty or risk is high [134]. Effective HITL systems balance automation with oversight by reserving human intervention for complex or high-stakes decisions, supported by clear role definitions, intuitive interfaces, escalation criteria, and continuous feedback loops. Diversity among human reviewers further reduces bias and strengthens accountability [135], [136]. Taken together, these findings indicate that robust LAUS should be designed as governed socio-technical systems rather than standalone intelligent agents. LLM observability provides transparency and quality control, safety verifiers enforce hard constraints, and HITL mechanisms supply ethical judgment and resilience, enabling UAV systems to scale intelligence while preserving safety, trust, and accountability in real-world deployment. VI. ICL-B ASED CASE STUDY FOR MULTI -UAV S YSTEMS 

This section transitions from theoretical frameworks to prac-tical validation by presenting an original case study on ICL for multi-UAV coordination. We demonstrate the application of an edge-hosted LLM to jointly optimize data-collection scheduling and velocity control in a dynamic emergency-response scenario. The case study details the system architecture, simulation environment, and operational workflow of the proposed ICL-based framework. Finally, we present comparative performance results against traditional RL baselines, empirically highlighting the advantages of LLM-assisted adaptability and real-time decision-making in UAV networks. This case study applies the ICL framework to multiple UAVs that jointly optimize data collection scheduling and velocity control. When multiple UAVs operate simultaneously, the actions of each UAV influence not only its own future states but also the behavior of other UAVs, resulting in a coupled multi-agent decision problem. In this scenario, each UAV interacts with an unknown environment and periodically transmits collected data and state information to an LLM. The LLM uses four key components – the task description ( T D task ), example set ( Ex t), current environment state ( et), and its internal reasoning – to generate the next action ( at). The task description encodes objectives, constraints, and expected outcomes, enabling operators to avoid explicitly designing optimization models. During operation, the LLM iteratively updates its decisions based on environment feedback, example demonstrations, and prior UAV actions, gradually converging on an effective policy. 

A. Multi-UAV Interaction and Decision Coupling 

In the multi-UAV scenario, each UAV independently selects sensors and adjusts its velocity. However, these decisions are interdependent and require a multi-agent ICL formulation to jointly minimize the total system cost, accounting for factors such as packet loss, buffer overflow, and failed transmissions. We propose the ICL-based Data Collection Scheduling and Velocity Control (DCS-ICL) framework for UASNets. DCS-ICL leverages ICL to optimize emergency data collection schedules and UAV velocities. Specifically, each UAV gathers sensory information, including queue lengths, battery levels, channel conditions, and UAV locations, from ground sensors and transmits this data to an edge-hosted LLM. The LLM is initialized with a structured system prompt that defines its operational role, constraints, and ethical safeguards, thereby ensuring compliance with safety and security requirements. Upon receiving environmental data, the LLM generates a detailed task description in natural language that includes mis-sion objectives, input parameters, operational rules, illustrative examples, expected outputs, and a feedback mechanism. In this context, the LLM infers an optimal data-collection schedule and velocity, which the UAVs then execute. Performance metrics and updated environmental states are continuously monitored and fed back into subsequent decision cycles, enabling dynamic adaptation and improvement of scheduling effectiveness over time. 

B. Simulation Setup 

We consider a scenario in which 10 ground sensors are randomly placed within a 100 m × 100 m area. Each sensor is configured with a maximum battery capacity of 50 J, a transmit queue limit of 40 packets, and a maximum transmit power of 

100 mW. In the learning configuration, each episode consists of 30 time steps. Simulations are performed in a customized Python 3.5 environment. UAV behavior is modeled according to the policies learned by the DCS-ICL framework. The wireless connection between UAVs and sensors is implemented using the probabilistic channel model. All experiments are conducted on a Lenovo workstation running Ubuntu 20.04 LTS, equipped with an Intel Core i5-7200U CPU @ 2.50 GHz × 4 and 16 GB of RAM. VII. M OST RECENT TRENDS 

The integration of LLMs into specialized domains, such as UAV networks, presents both unprecedented opportunities and complex challenges. Although current models demonstrate remarkable capabilities in natural language understanding, rea-soning, and autonomous decision-making, limitations persist in areas such as domain-specific expertise, efficiency, inter-pretability, and sustainability. Addressing these gaps requires targeted research that improves model performance while en-suring practical, ethical, and environmentally conscious de-ployment. The following directions highlight key avenues for advancing LLMs in UAVs and related applications, including improvements in specialization, efficiency, privacy, reliability, and integration with emerging technologies such as AGI and quantum computing. 

A. Near-Term Directions 1) Domain-Specific UAV LLMs for Autonomous Aerial Intel-ligence: A salient direction for future research is the develop-ment of fine-tuned, domain-specific LLMs explicitly designed for UAV applications. Drawing on industry trends in finance and healthcare, a specialized ”UAV-LLM” would be pretrained on aeronautical manuals, flight regulations, and telemetry data, then fine-tuned for mission-specific tasks such as trajectory planning, anomaly detection, and real-time decision-making. This deep specialization is expected to reduce hallucinations, improve accuracy, and provide a reliable cognitive core for UAV autonomy, enhancing operational safety, mission efficiency, and natural language interaction with ground control. Future research should also address the environmental and economic costs associated with large-scale LLMs. Rather than indiscriminate parameter scaling, efforts should focus on domain-specific optimization, carbon accountability, and strategic investment in practical AI applications. By combin-ing specialized model development with sustainable and cost-effective practices, UAV LLMs can deliver high performance and responsible deployment, ensuring scalable, trustworthy, and environmentally conscious aerial intelligence systems. 

2) Small and Edge-Deployable LLMs for UAV Applications: 

The growing viability of small LLMs offers a promising research direction for UAV applications. These compact models can run efficiently on CPUs or entirely on-device, significantly reducing inference costs, enhancing privacy, and enabling de-ployment in offline or secure environments. Techniques such as QLoRA, model distillation, and FlashAttention have improved the performance of small models, allowing them to match or surpass larger LLMs on targeted tasks while remaining lightweight and practical for real-world workloads. Future research should investigate the development of domain-specific, task-oriented LLMs optimized for UAV net-works that achieve high accuracy, reliability, and efficient resource use. Platforms such as Google’s AI Edge Gallery demonstrate the potential for running advanced LLMs locally on devices, supporting offline execution, low latency, and privacy-preserving operations [137]. Leveraging such edge-deployable LLMs could enable real-time onboard intelligence for flight control, mission planning, and sensor data processing. Further research should also examine sustainable AI practices that balance performance with environmental impact, as well as scalable deployment strategies to maximize ROI, accessi-bility, and operational efficiency in UAV and other resource-constrained domains. 

B. Medium-Term Directions 1) Self-Improving LLMs for UAV Applications: A promis-ing direction for future research is the development of self-improving LLMs capable of generating synthetic training data to address the critical shortage of domain-specific datasets in the UAV sector. By autonomously creating and fine-tuning on curated, self-generated question-answer pairs, these mod-els can achieve significant performance gains. In the UAV context, this approach could enable the generation of large-scale datasets covering operational scenarios, including nav-igation commands, anomaly detection procedures, and sensor data interpretation. Leveraging self-training capabilities enables continuous refinement of domain expertise, reduces reliance on costly manual data collection, and accelerates the development of highly specialized, efficient LLMs. Research in this area could unlock more advanced, autonomous aerial capabilities and serve as a blueprint for applying self-improving models in other data-scarce domains. 

2) Reducing Hallucinations in LLMs: Addressing halluci-nations in LLMs represents a critical area for future research. Hallucinations occur when models confidently generate plausi-ble but incorrect information and can be classified as extrinsic – misrecalling facts present in training data – or intrinsic – contradicting or exceeding the input context [138]. These errors often result from ambiguous prompts, noisy or limited training data, restricted context windows, and the model’s tendency to produce answers regardless of certainty. High-stakes domains, such as telecommunications, are particularly vulnerable to the consequences of hallucinations. Future research should explore strategies to mitigate these errors, such as grounding models in verified data sources, refining prompt design, decomposing complex tasks, leveraging domain-specific models, and integrat-ing human oversight. Advances in training methodologies, data integration, and evaluation frameworks could further enhance model reliability. Progress in these areas will be essential for developing trustworthy LLMs that consistently align with real-world knowledge and expectations. 

3) Towards Reliable LLM Systems:: A clear future research direction is the development of orchestration layers and system-level frameworks for LLMs. While current LLMs are already highly capable, their limitations in hallucination, reasoning, consistency, and context handling prevent their deployment in real-world applications with full reliability. Future research should focus on designing robust systems that manage, com-bine, and control LLMs, rather than simply scaling model size. Key areas include: a) Agent frameworks that enable LLMs to interact with tools, APIs, and other models in structured workflows, breaking complex tasks into verifiable subtasks; b) Reliability tooling, such as automated validators, fact-checkers, and monitoring systems, to detect and correct errors before outputs reach users; c) Safety and ethical guardrails that prevent biased, harmful, or unsafe outputs through constraint mechanisms, moderation layers, and verification pipelines; d) Workflow automation that integrates HITL checkpoints and ensures repeatable, traceable, and efficient processes for high-stakes tasks. Research in these areas will shift the focus from building ”bigger” models to creating trustworthy, production-ready LLM systems, enabling broader adoption in UAV networks while mitigating inherent limitations. The ultimate goal is to develop LLM-assisted systems that are not only fluent and capable but also reliable, safe, and self-regulating within a larger operational context. 

C. Long-term Directions 1) AGI-Enhanced LLMs: Advancing LLMs with AGI-inspired enhancements offers a promising direction for future research. Although transformer-based LLMs have demonstrated impressive abilities in language understanding and generation, they remain limited in reasoning, adaptability, and real-world comprehension. Research aimed at integrating AGI-driven cog-nitive frameworks could enable LLMs to incorporate symbolic reasoning, causal inference, and dynamic knowledge updates, supporting deeper conceptual understanding beyond statistical pattern recognition. These advancements could also improve bias mitigation, ethical governance, and multimodal reasoning, ultimately allowing LLMs to generalize knowledge more effec-tively across the UAV domain. 

2) Sustainable LLM Infrastructure: The high computational and energy demands of training and deploying LLMs remain a major constraint in the global AI landscape. For example, LLaMA-3-70B emits approximately 13 kg of CO 2 per 1million-token batch, while GPT-3’s training consumed 1,287 MWh – enough to power 120 U.S. households for a year. Inference adds further energy load, accounting for roughly 60% of total ML energy consumption. Regional factors, such as water-intensive cooling in Arizona or fossil-fuel-heavy elec-tricity grids in Chile, amplify environmental impacts. By 2030, AI could consume up to 10% of global electricity, exceeding the entire output of some countries, including Belgium. VIII. C ONCLUSION 

The integration of LLMs and MLLMs into UAV systems marks a significant step toward intelligent and adaptive aerial operations. This survey examined the convergence of LLM technologies with UAV communications, control, and ethical frameworks, providing a unified view of system architectures, methodologies, and real-world applications. By enabling ad-vanced reasoning, natural language interaction, and context-aware decision-making, LLMs support applications ranging from real-time SAR to swarm coordination and mission plan-ning. Techniques such as RAG and prompt engineering allow UAVs to operate dynamically in complex environments without extensive retraining, while MLLMs further extend these capa-bilities through multimodal perception and human-swarm inter-action. Despite their potential, LLM-assisted UAV systems raise critical challenges related to bias, transparency, accountability, and environmental impact. Addressing these issues requires robust governance mechanisms, including HITL oversight and LLM observability, to ensure safe and trustworthy deployment. Looking forward, key research directions include domain-specific UAV LLMs, efficient edge deployment, hallucination reduction, enhanced multimodal reasoning, and sustainable AI practices. The continued synergy among LLMs, 5G/6G connectivity, edge AI, and emerging computing paradigms is expected to accelerate the evolution of intelligent UAV ecosystems, enabling more reliable and responsible autonomous aerial systems for societal applications.
