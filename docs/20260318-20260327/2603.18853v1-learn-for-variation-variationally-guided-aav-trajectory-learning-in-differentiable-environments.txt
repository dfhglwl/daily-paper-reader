# Learn for Variation: Variationally Guided AAV Trajectory Learning in Differentiable Environments 

Xiucheng Wang, Zhenye Chen, Nan Cheng 

Abstract —Autonomous aerial vehicles (AAVs) empower sixth-generation (6G) Internet-of-Things (IoT) networks through mobility-driven data collection. However, conventional reward-driven reinforcement learning for AAV trajectory planning suffers from severe credit assignment issues and training instability, because sparse scalar rewards fail to capture the long-term and nonlinear effects of sequential movements. To address these challenges, this paper proposes Learn for Variation (L4V), a gradient-informed trajectory learning framework that replaces high-variance scalar reward signals with dense and analytically grounded policy gradients. Particularly, the coupled evolution of AAV kinematics, distance-dependent channel gains, and per-user data-collection progress is first unrolled into an end-to-end differentiable computational graph. Backpropagation through time then serves as a discrete adjoint solver, which propagates exact sensitivities from the cumulative mission objective to every control action and policy parameter. These structured gradients are used to train a deterministic neural policy with temporal smoothness regularization and gradient clipping. Exten-sive simulations demonstrate that L4V consistently outperforms representative baselines, including a genetic algorithm, DQN, A2C, and DDPG, in mission completion time, average transmission rate, and training cost 

Index Terms —Autonomous aerial vehicles, trajectory opti-mization, variational methods, differentiable simulation, adjoint gradients, neural policy learning. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Autonomous aerial vehicles (AAVs) are emerging as a key enabler for sixth-generation (6G) aerial-assisted Internet of Things (IoT) networks [1] by providing on-demand [2], [3] and reconfigurable wireless coverage over large areas [4]. By adjusting their three-dimensional positions, AAVs can reshape the radio link geometry [5], [6], create favorable propagation conditions, and deliver timely connectivity for data collection [7], hotspot offloading [8], and mission-driven sensing in environments where terrestrial infrastructure is unavailable [9]. This capability elevates mobility from a supporting feature to a primary design degree of freedom for network operation [10], [11], since the communication performance experienced by ground users becomes tightly coupled with the AAV flight trajectory over time. Accordingly, this work focuses on communication-aware trajectory planning, where

The central challenge is to coordinate sequential movement decisions with the accumulated communication utility over the entire mission horizon, rather than optimizing instantaneous metrics that may appear locally beneficial but lead to suboptimal system-level efficiency [1], [14]. Despite the apparent simplicity of commanding a moving platform for data collection [15], communication-aware trajec-tory planning is fundamentally difficult because the achievable data rate is a highly nonlinear [11], [16] and spatially localized function of the AAV position [17]. Small position changes can cause abrupt rate variations, and the service opportunity at each location depends sensitively on the distances to all ground users under heterogeneous task demands [7], [18]. More importantly, practical mission objectives are not determined by instantaneous link quality but by a time-accumulated utility that integrates data-collection progress and service continuity across the entire flight [19]. This long-horizon nature creates a severe credit-assignment difficulty: the value of a movement decision may only become apparent many steps later when the AAV reaches a strategically favorable region [20], [21]. Consequently, the learning signal derived from task outcomes is often sparse or delayed, leading to slow convergence and pronounced training instability for reward-driven methods, especially when deployment configurations change across scenarios. These challenges motivate a trajectory learning paradigm that can provide more informative optimization signals than end-of-horizon scalar rewards and can adapt to diverse operating conditions without extensive retraining [22]. Existing research has pursued two representative directions for communication-aware aerial decision making, yet neither fully resolves these difficulties [10], [20], [23]. Model-based trajectory design methods adopt simplified propagation models and quasi-static service assumptions to obtain tractable for-mulations and interpretable structural insights [24]. Although effective for revealing design trends and producing principled heuristics, their performance can degrade when missions involve spatially heterogeneous users and time-varying task demands [7]. Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) enables end-to-end decision making with limited reliance on explicit channel models, offering the potential to handle nonlinear dynamics and high-dimensional observations [3]. However, DRL methods typically demand large numbers of environment interactions, exhibit sensitivity to reward shaping, and suffer from training instability that is further amplified by long-horizon credit assignment and sparse returns [14]. A gap therefore persists between structured approaches that are difficult to adapt and

To bridge this gap, this paper develops Learn for Variation (L4V), a gradient-informed trajectory learning framework that injects first-order optimality information from variational principles into neural policy training. The key idea is to express the mission objective as a time-accumulated data-backlog cost and to unroll the entire flight evolution as an end-to-end differentiable computation graph, so that the influence of each movement decision on the final mission outcome can be traced through the underlying system dynamics. Backpropagation through time then serves as a discrete adjoint solver [25], [26], propagating exact sensitivities from the global objective to every control action and policy parameter. The resulting gradients are dense, low-variance, and structurally consistent with the communication mechanism, directly indicating how each local motion choice reshapes future service opportunities and long-term utility. These gradients replace the high-variance scalar reward signals of conventional policy gradient methods, thereby improving convergence speed and training stability. L4V further incorporates temporal smoothness regularization and norm-based gradient clipping to produce physically plau-sible trajectories and to prevent gradient explosion in long-horizon optimization. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows. 1) We formulate communication-aware AAV data collection as a discrete-time optimal control problem in which the mission objective is expressed as a cumulative data-backlog cost over the flight horizon. By deriving first-order necessary conditions via the Pontryagin minimum principle [26], the trajectory learning problem admits principled action-level supervision that goes beyond instantaneous greedy heuristics. 2) We construct an end-to-end differentiable simulation environment that captures the coupled evolution of AAV kinematics, distance-dependent channel gain, and per-user data-collection progress. The entire trajectory is unrolled as a differentiable computation graph, and backpropagation through time implements a discrete adjoint solver that yields exact, low-variance gradients of the global objective with respect to sequential actions and policy parameters. 3) We propose L4V, which integrates the resulting adjoint gradients into deterministic neural policy learning. Tem-poral smoothness regularization and norm-based gradient clipping are incorporated to improve trajectory regularity, convergence speed, and training stability in long-horizon settings. 4) We conduct systematic simulations against representative baselines spanning evolutionary optimization, value-based reinforcement learning, and actor-critic reinforce-ment learning, and vary user density, service-area size, unit-distance channel gain, and noise power. Results show that L4V consistently achieves lower mission completion time, higher average transmission rate, and reduced training cost in both iterations and wall-clock time, with the advantages widening under larger problem scales and more challenging channel conditions. Section VI reports simulation results and comparative evaluations. Section VII concludes the paper and discusses future directions. II. R ELATED WORKS 

The deployment of AAV in wireless networks transforms the static terrestrial infrastructure into a dynamic, three-dimensional ecosystem, introducing a trajectory optimization problem char-acterized by pathological non-convexity and high-dimensional state spaces. Existing literature has largely bifurcated into three distinct methodological streams: rigorous convex optimization, combinatorial graph theory, and data-driven reinforcement learning. The dominant framework for offline trajectory planning relies on Convex Optimization, prioritizing theoretical convergence and strict constraint satisfaction. Due to the tight coupling of trajectory variables, transmit power, and user scheduling within the Shannon capacity expression, block coordinate descent (BCD) is widely employed to decompose the master problem into tractable sub-problems [27]. To address the non-convexity arising from the Euclidean distance term in the channel model, successive convex approximation (SCA) is utilized to construct convex surrogates via first-order Taylor expansions, enabling the application of efficient interior-point solvers [28]. This BCD-SCA paradigm is particularly effective for maximizing max-min fairness, where the trajectory is optimized to serve bottleneck users by reformulating the objective via epigraph variables [29]. However, the computational intensity of solving quadratically constrained quadratic programs (QCQP) at each iteration renders this approach unsuitable for real-time applications [30]. Furthermore, these methods depend heavily on smooth, analytical channel models, failing to capture discrete environmental obstacles or complex multipath effects without oversimplified convex exclusion zones. To mitigate the computational burden of continuous opti-mization, a subset of literature discretizes the AAV mission into a sequence of logical decisions, leveraging Graph Theory. By abstracting hover locations as nodes, which are often derived via K-Means clustering or geometric intersection of communication disks [31], the trajectory problem is reduced to variants of the traveling salesman problem (TSP) [32] or vehicle routing problem (VRP) [33]. These methods excel in latency minimization and multi-UAV routing tasks, utilizing algorithms like Min-Max Cycle Cover to balance flight loads across a swarm. In obstructed urban environments, grid-based search algorithms such as A* and visibility graphs are employed to ensure collision-free routing. Despite their efficacy in global routing, graph-based methods inherently abstract away physical layer dynamics [34]. They typically assume constant velocities and simplified connectivity models, ignoring the potential throughput gains achievable through 3

velocity adaptation in fading channels. Consequently, graph solutions are often relegated to initialization roles for more granular solvers. The exigency for autonomy in dynamic, unknown environ-ments has driven the adoption of deep reinforcement learning (DRL) [35], framing trajectory design as a Markov decision process (MDP) [3]. Algorithms such as deep deterministic pol-icy gradient (DDPG) [36] and twin delayed DDPG (TD3) [37] enable UAVs to learn continuous control policies through trial-and-error, handling complex objectives that blend throughput, energy efficiency, and safety. TD3, in particular, mitigates the overestimation bias inherent in DDPG by employing clipped double Q-learning, yielding smoother and safer trajectories in obstacle-rich domains. For multi-agent scenarios, proximal policy optimization (PPO) is favored for its stability and resistance to catastrophic forgetting [38]. Nevertheless, standard DRL suffers from severe sample inefficiency and the sparse reward problem, where agents fail to converge without extensive reward shaping or curriculum learning. More critically, these model-free agents treat the wireless environment as a black box, ignoring the known physics of electromagnetic propagation, which leads to a significant performance gap when deploying policies trained on statistical models to physical reality. This context underscores the necessity of L4V. Unlike pure RL which ignores channel physics, or pure optimization which cannot adapt online, L4V is designed to bridge the gap between model-based rigor and data-driven flexibility. By integrating the physical constraints of the wireless medium directly into the learning representation, L4V addresses the limitations of high computational cost and the Sim-to-Real discrepancy, enabling trajectory optimization that is both physically compliant and operationally agile. B. Environment with Differentiable Transition 

The theoretical framework of the PMP provides the exact form of the gradients required for optimal control. However, to leverage these gradients for training a neural policy πθ , we must establish a practical mechanism for their computation. We achieve this by constructing the entire simulation environment as a single, end-to-end differentiable computational graph. This approach allows us to exploit the power of modern automatic differentiation (AD) frameworks. The key insight is that reverse-mode AD, when applied to a time-unrolled trajectory, is a direct computational implementation of the discrete adjoint method, thus enabling the calculation of the exact variational gradients. To construct this graph, every component of the simulation—from the system dynamics to the cost calculation—must be implemented using differentiable operations. VII. C ONCLUSION 

In this paper, we have proposed ”Learn for Variation” (L4V), a novel trajectory optimization framework that integrates variational optimal control theory with end-to-end differentiable simulation. By exploiting the reversibility of the computational graph, we have established a direct supervision mechanism that utilizes exact adjoint gradients to train neural policies, effectively overcoming the sample inefficiency and high variance limitations of reward-driven reinforcement learning. Simulation results demonstrate that the proposed approach significantly enhances the operational agility of practical AAV networks, achieving superior convergence speed and robust task completion rates compared to state-of-the-art baselines even in large-scale, stochastic environments. Future work will focus on extending this differentiable paradigm to decentralized multi-agent cooperative scenarios and validating the learned policies on hardware-in-the-loop experimental platforms.
